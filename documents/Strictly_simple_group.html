Strictly simple group

In mathematics, in the field of group theory, a group is said to be strictly simple if it has no proper nontrivial ascendant subgroups. That is, G G G is a strictly simple group if the only ascendant subgroups of G G G are { e } e \{e\} (the trivial subgroup), and G G G itself (the whole group).

In the finite case, a group is strictly simple if and only if it is simple. However, in the infinite case, strictly simple is a stronger property than simple.
