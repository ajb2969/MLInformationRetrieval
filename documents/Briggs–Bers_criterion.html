Briggs–Bers criterion

In stability theory, the Briggs–Bers criterion is a criterion for determining whether the trivial solution to a linear partial differential equation with constant coefficients is stable, convectively unstable or absolutely unstable. This is often useful in applied mathematics, especially in fluid dynamics, because linear PDEs often govern small perturbations to a system, and we are interested in whether such perturbations grow or decay. The Briggs–Bers criterion is named after R. J. Briggs and A. Bers.1

Transient growth

Suppose the PDE is of the form

y t = A y subscript y t A y y_{t}=Ay where A A A is a linear differential operator in x x x . In general, A A A is not a normal operator. While the large-time behaviour of y y y is still determined by the eigenvalues of A A A , the behaviour which takes place before this large-time behaviour may be dramatically different.4

In particular, while the eigenvalues of A A A may all have negative real part, which would predict that y y y decays exponentially at large times and that the trivial state y = 0 y 0 y=0 is stable, it is possible for y y y to grow transiently and become large before decaying.5 In practice, the linear equations that we work with are linearisations of more complicated governing equations such as the Navier–Stokes equations about some base state, with the linearisations carried out under the assumption that the perturbation quantity y y y is small. Transient growth may violate this assumption. When nonlinear effects are considered, then a system may be unstable even if the linearised system is stable.

Generalisation

When the coefficients of L L L vary with x x x , then this criterion is no longer applicable. However, if the variation is very slow, then the WKBJ approximation may be used to derive a leading-order approximation to the solution. This gives rise to the theory of global modes, which was first developed by Philip Drazin in 1974.6
