Puiseux series

In mathematics, Puiseux series are a generalization of power series, first introduced by Isaac Newton in 16761 and rediscovered by Victor Puiseux in 1850,2 that allows for negative and fractional exponents of the indeterminate T. A Puiseux series in the indeterminate T is a Laurent series in T1/n, where n is a positive integer. A Puiseux series may be written as:

∑ i = k ∞ a i T i / n , superscript subscript i k subscript a i superscript T i n \sum_{i=k}^{\infty}a_{i}T^{i/n}, where k k k is an integer and n n n is a positive integer.

Puiseux's theorem, sometimes also called Newton–Puiseux theorem, asserts that, given a polynomial equation P ( x , y ) = 0 P x y 0 P(x,y)=0 , its solutions in y y y , viewed as functions of x x x , may be expanded as Puiseux series that are convergent in some neighbourhood of the origin (0 excluded, in the case of a solution that tends to infinity at the origin). In other words, every branch of an algebraic curve may be locally (in terms of x x x ) described by a Puiseux series.

The set of Puiseux series over an algebraically closed field of characteristic 0 is itself an algebraically closed field, called the field of Puiseux series. It is the algebraic closure of the field of Laurent series. This statement is also referred to as Puiseux's theorem, being an expression of the original Puiseux theorem in modern abstract language.

Algebraic closedness of Puiseux series

One essential property of Puiseux series is expressed by the following theorem, attributed to Puiseux3 (for K = ℂ K ℂ K=\mathbb{C} ) but which was implicit in Newton's use of the Newton polygon as early as 16714 and therefore known either as Puiseux's theorem or as the Newton–Puiseux theorem:5

Theorem: if K is an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero, then the field of Puiseux series over K is the algebraic closure of the field of formal Laurent series over K.6

Very roughly, the proof proceeds essentially by inspecting the Newton polygon of the equation and extracting the coefficients one by one using a valuative form of Newton's method. Provided algebraic equations can be solved algorithmically in the base field K, then the coefficients of the Puiseux series solutions can be computed to any given order.

Similarly to the case of algebraic closure, there is an analogous theorem for real closure: if K is a real closed field, then the field of Puiseux series over K is the real closure of the field of formal Laurent series over K.8 (This implies the former theorem since any algebraically closed field of characteristic zero is the unique quadratic extension of some real-closed field.)

There is also an analogous result for p-adic closure: if K is a p-adically closed field with respect to a valuation w, then the field of Puiseux series over K is also p-adically closed.9

Puiseux expansion of algebraic curves and functions

Algebraic curves

Analytic convergence

When K = ℂ K ℂ K=\mathbb{C} , i.e. the field of complex numbers, the Puiseux expansion of an algebraic curve (as defined above) is convergent in the sense that for a given choice of n-th root of x, they converge for small enough | x | x |x| , hence define an analytic parametrization of each branch of X in the neighborhood of p (more precisely, the parametrization is by the n-th root of x).

Generalization

The field of Puiseux series is not complete, but its completion can be easily described: it is the field of formal expressions of the form f = ∑ e c e T e f subscript e subscript c e superscript T e f=\sum_{e}c_{e}T^{e}\, , where the support of the coefficients (that is, the set of e such that c e ≠ 0 subscript c e 0 c_{e}\neq 0 ) is the range of an increasing sequence of rational numbers that either is finite or tends to +∞. In other words, such series admit exponents of unbounded denominators, provided there are finitely many terms of exponent less than A for any given bound A. For example, ∑ k = 1 + ∞ T k + 1 k superscript subscript k 1 superscript T k 1 k \sum_{k=1}^{+\infty}T^{k+\frac{1}{k}} is not a Puiseux series, but it is the limit of a Cauchy sequence of Puiseux series (Puiseux polynomials). However, even this completion is still not "maximally complete" in the sense that it admits non-trivial extensions which are valued fields having the same value group and residue field,1415 hence the opportunity of completing it even more:

Hahn series are a further (larger) generalization of Puiseux series, introduced by Hans Hahn (in the course of the proof of his embedding theorem in 1907 and then studied by him in his approach to Hilbert's seventeenth problem), where instead of requiring the exponents to have bounded denominator they are required to form a well-ordered subset of the value group (usually ℚ ℚ \mathbb{Q} or ℝ ℝ \mathbb{R} ). These were later further generalized by Anatoly Maltsev and Bernhard Neumann to a non-commutative setting (they are therefore sometimes known as Hahn-Mal'cev-Neumann series). Using Hahn series, it is possible to give a description of the algebraic closure of the field of power series in positive characteristic which is somewhat analogous to the field of Puiseux series.16
