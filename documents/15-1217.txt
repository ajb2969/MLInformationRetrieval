   Matrix representation of Maxwell's equations      Matrix representation of Maxwell's equations   In electromagnetism , a branch of fundamental physics , the matrix representations of the Maxwell's equations are a formulation of Maxwell's equations using matrices , complex numbers , and vector calculus . These representations are for a homogeneous medium , an approximation in an inhomogeneous medium . A matrix representation for an inhomogeneous medium was presented using a pair of matrix equations. 1 A single equation using 4 Ã— 4 matrices is necessary and sufficient for any homogeneous medium. The Maxwell's equations have been expressed in a matrix form for a medium with varying permittivity Îµ = Îµ( r , t ) and permeability Î¼ = Î¼( r , t ), in presence of sources. This representation uses a single matrix equation, instead of a pair of matrix equations. In this representation, using 8 Ã— 8 matrices, it has been possible to separate the dependence of the coupling between the upper components (Î¨ + ) and the lower components (Î¨ âˆ’ ) through the two laboratory functions. Moreover the exact matrix representation has an algebraic structure very similar to the Dirac equation. 8 It is interesting to note that the Maxwell's equations can be derived from the Fermat's principle of geometrical optics by the process of "wavization" analogous to the quantization of classical mechanics . 9  Applications  One of the early uses of the matrix forms of the Maxwell's equations was to study certain symmetries, and the similarities with the Dirac equation.  The matrix form of the Maxwell's equations is used as a candidate for the Photon Wavefunction . 10  Historically, the geometrical optics is based on the Fermatâ€™s principle of least time . Geometrical optics can be completely derived from the Maxwell's equations. This is traditionally done using the Helmholtz equation . It is to be noted that the derivation of the Helmholtz equation from the Maxwellâ€™s equations is an approximation as one neglects the spatial and temporal derivatives of the permittivity and permeability of the medium. A new formalism of light beam optics has been developed, starting with the Maxwellâ€™s equations in a matrix form: a single entity containing all the four Maxwellâ€™s equations. Such a prescription is sure to provide a deeper understanding of beam-optics and polarization in a unified manner. 11 The beam-optical Hamiltonian derived from this matrix representation has an algebraic structure very similar to the Dirac equation , making it amenable to the Foldy-Wouthuysen technique . 12 This approach is very similar to one developed for the quantum theory of charged-particle beam optics. 13  References  Notes  Others   Bialynicki-Birula, I.
