   Conical surface      Conical surface   (Figure)  A circular conical surface   In geometry , a ( general ) conical surface is the unbounded surface formed by the union of all the straight lines that pass through a fixed point â€” the apex or vertex â€” and any point of some fixed space curve â€” the directrix â€” that does not contain the apex. Each of those lines is called a generatrix of the surface.  Every conic surface is ruled and developable . In general, a conical surface consists of two congruent unbounded halves joined by the apex. Each half is called a nappe , and is the union of all the rays that start at the apex and pass through a point of some fixed space curve. (In some cases, however, the two nappes may intersect, or even coincide with the full surface.) Sometimes the term "conical surface" is used to mean just one nappe. This special case is often called a cone , because it is one of the two distinct surfaces that bound the geometric solid of that name.
