Angles between flats

The concept of angles between lines in the plane and between pairs of two lines, two planes or a line and a plane in space can be generalised to arbitrary dimension. This generalisation was first discussed by Jordan.1 For any pair of flats in a Euclidean space of arbitrary dimension one can define a set of mutual angles which are invariant under isometric transformation of the Euclidean space. If the flats do not intersect, their shortest distance is one more invariant.2 These angles are called canonical3 or principal.4 The concept of angles can be generalised to pairs of flats in a finite-dimensional inner product space over the complex numbers.

Basic properties

If the largest angle is zero, one subspace is a subset of the other.

If the smallest angle is zero, the subspaces intersect at least in a line.

The number of angles equal to zero is the dimension of the space where the two subspaces intersect.
