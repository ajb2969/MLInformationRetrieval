Mean inter-particle distance

Mean inter-particle distance (or mean inter-particle separation) is the mean distance between microscopic particles (usually atoms or molecules) in a macroscopic body.

Ambiguity

From the very general considerations, the mean inter-particle distance is proportional to the size of the per-particle volume 1 / n 1 n 1/n , i.e.,

One such an estimation is the Wigner-Seitz radius

( 3 4 π n ) 1 / 3 , superscript 3 4 π n 1 3 \left(\frac{3}{4\pi n}\right)^{1/3}, which corresponds to the radius of a sphere having per-particle volume 1 / n 1 n 1/n . Another popular definition is

1 / n 1 / 3 1 superscript n 1 3 1/n^{1/3} , corresponding to the length of the edge of the cube with the per-particle volume 1 / n 1 n 1/n . On the other hand, it is often used in qualitative statements where such a numeric factor is either irrelevant or plays an insignificant role, e.g.,

  • "a potential energy ... is proportional to some power n of the inter-particle distance r" (Virial theorem)
  • "the inter-particle distance is much larger than the thermal de Broglie wavelength" (Kinetic theory)
