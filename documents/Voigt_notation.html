Voigt notation

In mathematics, Voigt notation or Voigt form in multilinear algebra is a way to represent a symmetric tensor by reducing its order. There are a few variants and associated names for this idea: Mandel notation, Mandel‚ÄìVoigt notation and Nye notation are others found. Kelvin notation is a revival by Helbig (1994) of old ideas of Lord Kelvin. The differences here lie in certain weights attached to the selected entries of the tensor. Nomenclature may vary according to what is traditional in the field of application.

Mnemonic rule

A simple mnemonic rule for memorizing Voigt notation is as follows:

  • Write down the second order tensor in matrix form (in the example, the stress tensor)
  • Strike out the diagonal
  • Continue on the third column
  • Go back to the first element along the first row.

Voigt indexes are numbered consecutively from the starting point to the end (in the example, the numbers in blue).

Applications

The notation is named after physicist Woldemar Voigt. It is useful, for example, in calculations involving constitutive models to simulate materials, such as the generalized Hooke's law, as well as finite element analysis.

Hooke's law has a symmetric fourth-order stiffness tensor with 81 components (3√ó3√ó3√ó3). Voigt notation enables this to be simplified to a 6√ó6 matrix. However, Voigt's form does not preserve the sum of the squares, which in the case of Hooke's law has geometric significance. This explains why weights are introduced (to make the mapping an isometry).

A discussion of invariance of Voigt's notation and Mandel's notation be found in Helnwein (2001).
