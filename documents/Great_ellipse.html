Great ellipse

right|150px|thumb|A spheroid

A great ellipse is an ellipse passing through two points on a spheroid and having the same center as that of the spheroid. Equivalently, it is an ellipse on the surface of spheroid and centered at the origin, or the curve formed by intersecting the spheroid by a plane through its center.1 For points which are separated by less than about a quarter of the circumference of the earth, about 10 000 km 10 000 km 10\,000\,\mathrm{km} , the length of the great ellipse connecting the points is close (within one part in 500,000) to the geodesic distance.234 The great ellipse therefore is sometimes proposed as a suitable route for marine navigation.

There are various ways to map an ellipsoid into a sphere of radius a a a in such a way as to map the great ellipse into a great circle, allowing the methods of great-circle navigation to be used:

  • The ellipsoid can be stretched in a direction parallel to the axis of rotation; this maps a point of latitude ϕ ϕ \phi on the ellipsoid to a point on the sphere with latitude β β \beta , the parametric latitude.
  • A point on the ellipsoid can mapped radially onto the sphere along the line connecting it with the center of the ellipsoid; this maps a point of latitude ϕ ϕ \phi on the ellipsoid to a point on the sphere with latitude θ θ \theta , the geocentric latitude.
  • The ellipsoid can be stretched into a prolate ellipsoid with polar semi-axis a 2 / b superscript a 2 b a^{2}/b and then mapped radially onto the sphere; this preserves the latitude—the latitude on the sphere is ϕ ϕ \phi , the geographic latitude.

The last method gives an easy way to generate a succession of way-points on the great ellipse connecting two known points A A A and B B B .
