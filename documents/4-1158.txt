   Laguerre polynomials      Laguerre polynomials   In mathematics , the Laguerre polynomials , named after Edmond Laguerre (1834 - 1886), are solutions of Laguerre's equation:        x   y  ′′    +    (   1  -  x   )    y  ′    +   n  y    =  0          x   superscript  y  ′′        1  x    superscript  y  normal-′      n  y    0    xy^{\prime\prime}+(1-x)y^{\prime}+ny=0     which is a second-order linear differential equation . This equation has nonsingular solutions only if n is a non-negative integer.  More generally, the name Laguerre polynomials is used for solutions of         x   y  ′′    +    (    α  +  1   -  x   )    y  ′    +   n  y    =   0    .          x   superscript  y  ′′          α  1   x    superscript  y  normal-′      n  y    0    xy^{\prime\prime}+(\alpha+1-x)y^{\prime}+ny=0~{}.     Then they are also named generalized Laguerre polynomials , as will be done here (alternatively associated Laguerre polynomials or, rarely, Sonin polynomials , after their inventor 1  Nikolay Yakovlevich Sonin ). The Rook polynomials in combinatorics are more or less the same as Laguerre polynomials, up to elementary changes of variables. Further see the Tricomi–Carlitz polynomials .  The Laguerre polynomials arise in quantum mechanics, in the radial part of the solution of the Schrödinger equation for a one-electron atom. They also describe the static Wigner functions of oscillator systems in quantum mechanics in phase space . They further enter in the quantum mechanics of the Morse potential and of the 3D isotropic harmonic oscillator .  Physicists sometimes use a definition for the Laguerre polynomials which is larger by a factor of n ! than the definition used here. (Likewise, some physicists may use somewhat different definitions of the so-called associated Laguerre polynomials.)
