Hubble volume

In cosmology, a Hubble volume, or Hubble sphere, is a spherical region of the Universe surrounding an observer beyond which objects recede from that observer at a rate greater than the speed of light due to the expansion of the Universe.1 The Hubble volume is approximately 1031 cubic light years.2

The surface of a Hubble sphere is called the microphysical horizon,3 the Hubble surface, or the Hubble limit.

More generally, the term "Hubble volume" can be applied to any region of space with a volume of order ( c / H 0 ) 3 superscript c subscript H 0 3 (c/H_{0})^{3} . However, the term is also frequently (but mistakenly) used as a synonym for the observable universe; the latter is larger than the Hubble volume.45

Relationship to age of the universe

The Hubble length c / H 0 c subscript H 0 c/H_{0} is 14 billion light years in the standard cosmological model, somewhat larger than c c c times the age of the universe, 13.8 billion years. This is because 1 / H 0 1 subscript H 0 1/H_{0} gives the age of the universe by assuming that each galaxy have been receding from the Milky Way at the same speed throughout the age of the universe, and considers the speed. However, this simplified model differs from the Big Bang model in several ways. Firstly, the Big Bang model does not assume that we are at the centre of the universe, or that there is any position in space that can claim to be the `centre' of the Big Bang. Secondly, Hubble's law with a Hubble Constant that does not change with time predicts that a galaxy would accelerate away from an observer, since the recession velocity increases with distance. Lastly, recent research suggests that the Hubble constant changes with time,6 due to dark energy, so that 1 / H 0 1 subscript H 0 1/H_{0} is only an approximation to the age of the universe.

Hubble limit as an event horizon

Objects at the Hubble limit have an average proper speed of c relative to an observer on the Earth so that, in a universe with constant Hubble parameter, light emitted at the present time by objects outside the Hubble limit would never be seen by an observer on Earth. That is, Hubble limit would coincide with a cosmological event horizon (a boundary separating events visible at some time and those that are never visible7).

However, the Hubble parameter is not constant in various cosmological models8 so that the Hubble limit does not, in general, coincide with a cosmological event horizon. For example in a decelerating Friedmann universe the Hubble sphere expands faster than the Universe and its boundary overtakes light emitted by receding galaxies so that light emitted at earlier times by objects outside the Hubble sphere still may eventually arrive inside the sphere and be seen by us.9 Conversely, in an accelerating universe, the Hubble sphere expands more slowly than the Universe, and bodies move out of the Hubble sphere.10

Observations indicate that the universe is accelerating,11 so that some objects that we can currently exchange signals with will one day cross our Hubble limit.
