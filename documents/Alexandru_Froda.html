Alexandru Froda

Alexandru Froda (July 16, 1894, Bucharest, Romania – October 7, 1973, Bucharest, Romania) was a well-known Romanian mathematician with important contributions in the field of mathematical analysis, algebra, number theory and rational mechanics. In his 1929 thesis he proved what is now known as Froda's theorem.1

Life

Alexandru Froda was born in Bucharest in 1894. In 1927 he graduated from the University of Sciences (now the Faculty of Mathematics from the University of Bucharest). He received his Ph.D. from the University of Paris and from University of Bucharest in 1929. He was elected president of the Romanian Mathematical Society in 1946. In 1948 he became professor at the Faculty of Mathematics and Physics at the University of Bucharest.

Work

Froda's major contribution was in the field of mathematical analysis. His first important result2 was concerned with the set of discontinuities of a real-valued function of a real variable. In this theorem Froda proves that the set of simple discontinuities of a real-valued function of a real variable is at most countable.

In a paper3 from 1936 he proved a necessary and sufficient condition for a function to be measurable.

In the theory of algebraic equations Froda proved4 a method of solving algebraic equations having complex coefficients.

In 1929 Dimitrie Pompeiu conjectured that any continuous function of two real variables defined on the entire plane is constant if the integral over any circle in the plane is constant. In the same year5 Froda proved that, in the case that the conjecture is true, the condition that the function is defined on the whole plane is indispensable. Later it was shown that the conjecture is not true in general.

In 1907 D. Pompeiu constructed an example of a continuous function with a nonzero derivative which has a zero in every interval. Using this result Froda finds a new way of looking at an older problem6 posed by Mikhail Lavrentyev in 1925, namely whether there is a function of two real variables such that the ordinary differential equation d y = f ( x , y ) d x d y f x y d x dy=f(x,y)dx has at least two solutions passing through every point in the plane.

In the theory of numbers, beside rational triangles7 he also proved several conditions89101112 for a real number, which is the limit of a rational convergent sequence, to be irrational, extending a previous result of Viggo Brun from 1910.13

In 1937 Froda independently noticed and proved the case n = 1 n 1 n=1 of the Borsuk-Ulam theorem.
