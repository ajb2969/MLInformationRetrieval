The potentials under which the theorem holds are defined up to an additive purely time-dependent function: such functions only change the phase of the wavefunction and leave the density invariant. Most often the RG theorem is applied to molecular systems where the electronic density , Ï ( r , t ) changes in response to an external scalar potential , v ( r , t ), such as a time-varying electric field. 1  The Rungeâ€“Gross theorem provides the formal foundation of time-dependent density functional theory. It shows that the density can be used as the fundamental variable in describing quantum many-body systems in place of the wavefunction, and that all properties of the system are functionals of the density. The proof also assumes that the density vanishes at infinity, making it valid only for finite systems. Given this result, the continuity equation is used in a second step to relate time-derivatives of the electronic density to time-derivatives of the external potential.
