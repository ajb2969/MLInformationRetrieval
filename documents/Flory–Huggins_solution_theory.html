Flory–Huggins solution theory

Flory–Huggins solution theory' is a mathematical model of the thermodynamics of polymer solutions which takes account of the great dissimilarity in molecular sizes in adapting the usual expression for the entropy of mixing. The result is an equation for the Gibbs free energy change Δ G m normal-Δ subscript G m \Delta G_{m} for mixing a polymer with a solvent. Although it makes simplifying assumptions, it generates useful results for interpreting experiments. The thermodynamic equation for the Gibbs free energy change accompanying mixing at constant temperature and (external) pressure is

Δ G m = Δ H m - T Δ S m normal-Δ subscript G m normal-Δ subscript H m T normal-Δ subscript S m \Delta G_{m}=\Delta H_{m}-T\Delta S_{m}\,

A change, denoted by Δ normal-Δ \Delta , is the value of a variable for a solution or mixture minus the values for the pure components considered separately. The objective is to find explicit formulas for Δ H m normal-Δ subscript H m \Delta H_{m} and Δ S m normal-Δ subscript S m \Delta S_{m} , the enthalpy and entropy increments associated with the mixing process.

The result obtained by Flory and Huggins is

Δ G m = R T [ n 1 ln ϕ 1 + n 2 ln ϕ 2 + n 1 ϕ 2 χ 12 ] normal-Δ subscript G m R T delimited-[] subscript n 1 subscript ϕ 1 subscript n 2 subscript ϕ 2 subscript n 1 subscript ϕ 2 subscript χ 12 \Delta G_{m}=RT[\,n_{1}\ln\phi_{1}+n_{2}\ln\phi_{2}+n_{1}\phi_{2}\chi_{12}\,]\,

The right-hand side is a function of the number of moles n 1 subscript n 1 n_{1} and volume fraction ϕ 1 subscript ϕ 1 \phi_{1} of solvent (component  1 1 1 ), the number of moles n 2 subscript n 2 n_{2} and volume fraction ϕ 2 subscript ϕ 2 \phi_{2} of polymer (component  2 2 2 ), with the introduction of a parameter χ χ \chi to take account of the energy of interdispersing polymer and solvent molecules. R R R is the gas constant and T T T is the absolute temperature. The volume fraction is analogous to the mole fraction, but is weighted to take account of the relative sizes of the molecules. For a small solute, the mole fractions would appear instead, and this modification is the innovation due to Flory and Huggins. It should be noted that in the most general case the mixing parameter, χ χ \chi , is a free energy parameter, thus including an entropic component. 12

Derivation

We first calculate the entropy of mixing, the increase in the uncertainty about the locations of the molecules when they are interspersed. In the pure condensed phases — solvent and polymer — everywhere we look we find a molecule. Of course, any notion of "finding" a molecule in a given location is a thought experiment since we can't actually examine spatial locations the size of molecules. The expression for the entropy of mixing of small molecules in terms of mole fractions is no longer reasonable when the solute is a macromolecular chain. We take account of this dissymmetry in molecular sizes by assuming that individual polymer segments and individual solvent molecules occupy sites on a lattice. Each site is occupied by exactly one molecule of the solvent or by one monomer of the polymer chain, so the total number of sites is

N = N 1 + x N 2 N subscript N 1 x subscript N 2 N=N_{1}+xN_{2}\, N 1 subscript N 1 N_{1} is the number of solvent molecules and N 2 subscript N 2 N_{2} is the number of polymer molecules, each of which has x x x segments.

From statistical mechanics we can calculate the entropy change, the increase in spatial uncertainty, as a result of mixing solute and solvent.

Δ S m = - k [ N 1 ln ( N 1 / N ) + N 2 ln ( x N 2 / N ) ] normal-Δ subscript S m k delimited-[] subscript N 1 subscript N 1 N subscript N 2 x subscript N 2 N \Delta S_{m}=-k[\,N_{1}\ln(N_{1}/N)+N_{2}\ln(xN_{2}/N)\,]\,

where k k k is Boltzmann's constant. Define the lattice volume fractions ϕ 1 subscript ϕ 1 \phi_{1} and ϕ 2 subscript ϕ 2 \phi_{2}

ϕ 1 = N 1 / N subscript ϕ 1 subscript N 1 N \phi_{1}=N_{1}/N\,

ϕ 2 = x N 2 / N subscript ϕ 2 x subscript N 2 N \phi_{2}=xN_{2}/N\,

These are also the probabilities that a given lattice site, chosen at random, is occupied by a solvent molecule or a polymer segment, respectively. Thus

Δ S m = - k [ N 1 ln ϕ 1 + N 2 ln ϕ 2 ] normal-Δ subscript S m k delimited-[] subscript N 1 subscript ϕ 1 subscript N 2 subscript ϕ 2 \Delta S_{m}=-k[\,N_{1}\ln\phi_{1}+N_{2}\ln\phi_{2}\,]\,

For a small solute whose molecules occupy just one lattice site, x x x equals one, the volume fractions reduce to molecular or mole fractions, and we recover the usual equation from ideal mixing theory.

In addition to the entropic effect, we can expect an enthalpy change. There are three molecular interactions to consider: solvent-solvent w 11 subscript w 11 w_{11} , monomer-monomer w 22 subscript w 22 w_{22} (not the covalent bonding, but between different chain sections), and monomer-solvent w 12 subscript w 12 w_{12} . That is, x N 2 x subscript N 2 xN_{2} is the total number of polymer segments (monomers) in the solution, so x N 2 z x subscript N 2 z xN_{2}z is the number of nearest-neighbor sites to all the polymer segments. An approximation following mean field theory is made by following this procedure, thereby reducing the complex problem of many interactions to a simpler problem of one interaction.

The value of the interaction parameter can be estimated from the Hildebrand solubility parameters δ a subscript δ a \delta_{a} and δ b subscript δ b \delta_{b}

χ 12 = V s e g ( δ a - δ b ) 2 / R T subscript χ 12 subscript V s e g superscript subscript δ a subscript δ b 2 R T \chi_{12}=V_{seg}(\delta_{a}-\delta_{b})^{2}/RT\,

where V s e g subscript V s e g V_{seg} is the actual volume of a polymer segment.

This treatment does not attempt to calculate the conformational entropy of folding for polymer chains. (See the random coil discussion.) The conformations of even amorphous polymers will change when they go into solution, and most thermoplastic polymers also have lamellar crystalline regions which do not persist in solution as the chains separate. These events are accompanied by additional entropy and energy changes.

It should be noted that in the most general case the interaction Δ w normal-Δ w \Delta w and the ensuing mixing parameter, χ χ \chi , is a free energy parameter, thus including an entropic component. 34 This means that aside to the regular mixing entropy there is an another entropic contribution from the interaction between solvent and monomer. This contribution is sometimes very important in order to make quantitative predictions of thermodynamic properties.

More advanced solution theories exist, such as the Flory-Krigbaum theory.
