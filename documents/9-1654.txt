   Curvature of a measure      Curvature of a measure   In mathematics , the curvature of a measure defined on the Euclidean plane  R 2 is a quantification of how much the measure's "distribution of mass" is "curved". It is related to notions of curvature in geometry . In the form presented below, the concept was introduced in 1995 by the mathematician  Mark S. Melnikov ; accordingly, it may be referred to as the Melnikov curvature or Menger-Melnikov curvature . Examples   The trivial measure has zero curvature.  A Dirac measure  Î´ a supported at any point a has zero curvature.  If Î¼ is any measure whose support is contained within a Euclidean line L , then Î¼ has zero curvature. For example, one-dimensional Lebesgue measure on any line (or line segment) has zero curvature.  The Lebesgue measure defined on all of R 2 has infinite curvature.  If Î¼ is the uniform one-dimensional Hausdorff measure on a circle C r or radius r , then Î¼ has curvature 1/ r .
