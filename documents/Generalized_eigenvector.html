Generalized eigenvector

In linear algebra, a generalized eigenvector of an n × n matrix A A A is a vector which satisfies certain criteria which are more relaxed than those for an (ordinary) eigenvector.1

Let V V V be an n-dimensional vector space; let ϕ ϕ \phi be a linear map in L ( V ) L V L(V) , the set of all linear maps from V V V into itself; and let A A A be the matrix representation of ϕ ϕ \phi with respect to some ordered basis.

There may not always exist a full set of n linearly independent eigenvectors of A A A that form a complete basis for V V V . That is, the matrix A A A may not be diagonalizable.23 This happens when the algebraic multiplicity of at least one eigenvalue λ i subscript λ i \lambda_{i} is greater than its geometric multiplicity (the nullity of the matrix ( A - λ i I ) A subscript λ i I (A-\lambda_{i}I) , or the dimension of its nullspace). In this case, λ i subscript λ i \lambda_{i} is called a defective eigenvalue and A A A is called a defective matrix.4

A generalized eigenvector x i subscript x i x_{i} corresponding to λ i subscript λ i \lambda_{i} , together with the matrix ( A - λ i I ) A subscript λ i I (A-\lambda_{i}I) generate a Jordan chain of linearly independent generalized eigenvectors which form a basis for an invariant subspace of V V V .567

Using generalized eigenvectors, a set of linearly independent eigenvectors of A A A can be extended, if necessary, to a complete basis for V V V .8 This basis can be used to determine an "almost diagonal matrix" J J J in Jordan normal form, similar to A A A , which is useful in computing certain matrix functions of A A A .9 The matrix J J J is also useful in solving the system of linear differential equations 𝕩 ′ = A 𝕩 , superscript 𝕩 normal-′ A 𝕩 \mathbb{x}^{\prime}=A\mathbb{x}, where A A A need not be diagonalizable.1011

Overview and definition

There are several equivalent ways to define an ordinary eigenvector.1213141516171819 For our purposes, an eigenvector 𝕦 𝕦 \mathbb{u} associated with an eigenvalue λ λ \lambda of an n n n × n n n matrix A A A is a nonzero vector for which ( A - λ I ) 𝕦 = 𝟘 A λ I 𝕦 0 (A-\lambda I)\mathbb{u}=\mathbb{0} , where I I I is the n n n × n n n identity matrix and 𝟘 0 \mathbb{0} is the zero vector of length n n n .20 That is, 𝕦 𝕦 \mathbb{u} is in the kernel of the transformation ( A - λ I ) A λ I (A-\lambda I) . If A A A has n n n linearly independent eigenvectors, then A A A is similar to a diagonal matrix D D D . That is, there exists an invertible matrix M M M such that A A A is diagonalizable through the similarity transformation D = M - 1 A M D superscript M 1 A M D=M^{-1}AM .2122 The matrix D D D is called a spectral matrix for A A A . The matrix M M M is called a modal matrix for A A A .23 Diagonalizable matrices are of particular interest since matrix functions of them can be computed easily.24

On the other hand, if A A A does not have n n n linearly independent eigenvectors associated with it, then A A A is not diagonalizable.2526

Clearly, a generalized eigenvector of rank 1 is an ordinary eigenvector.28 Every n n n × n n n matrix A A A has n n n linearly independent generalized eigenvectors associated with it and can be shown to be similar to an "almost diagonal" matrix J J J in Jordan normal form.29 That is, there exists an invertible matrix M M M such that J = M - 1 A M J superscript M 1 A M J=M^{-1}AM .30 The matrix M M M in this case is called a generalized modal matrix for A A A .31 If λ λ \lambda is an eigenvalue of algebraic multiplicity μ μ \mu , then A A A will have μ μ \mu linearly independent generalized eigenvectors corresponding to λ λ \lambda .32 These results, in turn, provide a straightforward method for computing certain matrix functions of A A A .33

Generalized modal matrix

Let A A A be an n × n matrix. A generalized modal matrix M M M for A A A is an n × n matrix whose columns, considered as vectors, form a canonical basis for A A A and appear in M M M according to the following rules:

  • All Jordan chains consisting of one vector (that is, one vector in length) appear in the first columns of M M M .
  • All vectors of one chain appear together in adjacent columns of M M M .
  • Each chain appears in M M M in order of increasing rank (that is, the generalized eigenvector of rank 1 appears before the generalized eigenvector of rank 2 of the same chain, which appears before the generalized eigenvector of rank 3 of the same chain, etc.).45
