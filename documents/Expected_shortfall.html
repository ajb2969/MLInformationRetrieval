Expected shortfall

Expected shortfall (ES) is a risk measure, a concept used in finance (and more specifically in the field of financial risk measurement) to evaluate the market risk or credit risk of a portfolio. It is an alternative to value at risk that is more sensitive to the shape of the loss distribution in the tail of the distribution. The "expected shortfall at q% level" is the expected return on the portfolio in the worst q q q % of the cases.

Expected shortfall is also called conditional value at risk (CVaR), average value at risk (AVaR), and expected tail loss (ETL).

ES evaluates the value (or risk) of an investment in a conservative way, focusing on the less profitable outcomes. For high values of q q q it ignores the most profitable but unlikely possibilities, for small values of q q q it focuses on the worst losses. On the other hand, unlike the discounted maximum loss even for lower values of q q q expected shortfall does not consider only the single most catastrophic outcome. A value of q q q often used in practice is 5%.

Expected shortfall is a coherent, and moreover a spectral, measure of financial portfolio risk. It requires a quantile-level q q q , and is defined to be the expected loss of portfolio value given that a loss is occurring at or below the q q q -quantile.

Properties

The expected shortfall E S q E subscript S q ES_{q} increases as q q q increases.

The 100%-quantile expected shortfall E S 1.0 E subscript S 1.0 ES_{1.0} equals the expected value of the portfolio.

For a given portfolio, the expected shortfall E S q E subscript S q ES_{q} is greater than or equal to the Value at Risk VaR q subscript VaR q \operatorname{VaR}_{q} at the same q q q level.
