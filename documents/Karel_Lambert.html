Karel Lambert

Karel Lambert (born 1928) is a philosopher and logician at the University of California, Irvine and the University of Salzburg. He has written extensively on the subject of free logic, a term which he coined.1

Lambert's Law

Lambert's Law is the major principle in any free definite description theory that says: For all x, x = the y (A) if and only if (A(x/y) & for all y (if A then y = x)). Free logic itself is an adjustment of a given standard predicate logic such as to relieve it of existential assumptions, and so make it a free logic. Taking Bertrand Russell's predicate logic in his Principia Mathematica as standard, one replaces universal instantiation, ∀ x ϕ x → ϕ y normal-→ for-all x ϕ x ϕ y \forall x\,\phi x\rightarrow\phi y , with universal specification ( ∀ x ϕ x and E ! y ϕ y ) → ϕ z normal-→ for-all x ϕ x italic- and E y ϕ y ϕ z (\forall x\,\phi x\and E!y\,\phi y)\rightarrow\phi z . Thus universal statements, like "All men are mortal," or "Everything is a unicorn," do not presuppose that there are men or that there is anything. These would be symbolized, with the appropriate predicates, as ∀ x ( M x → L x ) fragments for-all x fragments normal-( M x normal-→ L x normal-) \forall x\,(Mx\rightarrow Lx) and ∀ x U x for-all x U x \forall x\,Ux , which in Principia Mathematica entail ∃ x ( M x and L x ) x M x italic- and L x \exists x\,(Mx\and Lx) and ∃ x U x x U x \exists x\,Ux , but not in free logic. The truth of these last statements, when used in a free logic, depend on the domain of quantification, which may be the null set.
