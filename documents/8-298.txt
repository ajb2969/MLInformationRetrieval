   Poussin proof      Poussin proof   In number theory , the Poussin proof is the proof of an identity related to the fractional part of a ratio.  In 1838, Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet proved an approximate formula for the average number of divisors of all the numbers from 1 to η:          ∑   k  =  1   η    d   (  k  )     η   ≈     ln  η   +   2  γ    -  1    ,          superscript   subscript     k  1    η     d  k    η         η     2  γ    1     \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{\eta}d(k)}{\eta}\approx\ln\eta+2\gamma-1,     where d represents the divisor function , and γ represents the Euler-Mascheroni constant .  In 1898, Charles Jean de la Vallée-Poussin proved that if a large number η is divided by all the primes up to η, then the average fraction by which the quotient falls short of the next whole number is γ:          ∑   p  ≤  η     {   η  p   }     π   (  η  )     ≈   1  -  γ    ,          subscript     p  η       η  p       π  η      1  γ     \frac{\sum_{p\leq\eta}\left\{\frac{\eta}{p}\right\}}{\pi(\eta)}\approx 1-\gamma,   where { x } represents the fractional part of x , and π represents the prime-counting function . For example, if we divide 29 by 2, we get 14.5, which falls short of 15 by 0.5.  References   Dirichlet, G. L.
