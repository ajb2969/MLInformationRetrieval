Convex position

In discrete and computational geometry, a set of points in the Euclidean plane is said to be in convex position or convex independent if none of the points can be represented as a convex combination of the others.1 A finite set of points is in convex position if all of the points are vertices of their convex hull.2 More generally, a family of convex sets is said to be in convex position if they are pairwise disjoint and none of them is contained in the convex hull of the others.3

An assumption of convex position can make certain computational problems easier to solve. For instance, the traveling salesman problem, NP-hard for arbitrary sets of points in the plane, is trivial for points in convex position: the optimal tour is the convex hull.4 Similarly, the minimum-weight triangulation is NP-hard for arbitrary point sets,5 but solvable in polynomial time by dynamic programming for points in convex position.6
