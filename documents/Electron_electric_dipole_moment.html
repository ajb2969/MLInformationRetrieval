The electron's EDM must be collinear with the direction of the electron's magnetic moment (spin).1 Within the standard model of elementary particle physics, such a dipole is predicted to be non-zero but very small, at most ,2 where e stands for the elementary charge. The existence of a non-zero electron electric dipole moment would imply a violation of both parity invariance and time reversal invariance.3 In the Standard Model, the electron EDM arises from the CP-violating components of the CKM matrix. The moment is very small because the CP violation involves quarks, not electrons directly, so it can only arise by quantum processes where virtual quarks are created, interact with the electron, and then are annihilated.4 More precisely, a non-zero EDM does not arise until the level of four-loop Feynman diagrams and higher.5 An additional, larger EDM (around ) is possible in the standard model if neutrinos are majorana particles.6
