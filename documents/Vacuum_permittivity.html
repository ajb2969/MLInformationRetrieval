Terminology

Historically, the parameter Îµ0 has been known by many different names. The terms "vacuum permittivity" or its variants, such as "permittivity in/of vacuum",910 "permittivity of empty space",11 or "permittivity of free space"12 are widespread. Standards Organizations worldwide now use "electric constant" as a uniform term for this quantity,13 and official standards documents have adopted the term (although they continue to list the older terms as synonyms).1415

Another historical synonym was "dielectric constant of vacuum", as "dielectric constant" was sometimes used in the past for the absolute permittivity.1617 However, in modern usage "dielectric constant" typically refers exclusively to a relative permittivity Îµ/Îµ0 and even this usage is considered "obsolete" by some standards bodies in favor of relative static permittivity.1819 Hence, the term "dielectric constant of vacuum" for the electric constant Îµ0 is considered obsolete by most modern authors, although occasional examples of continuing usage can be found.

Historical origin of the parameter Îµ0

As indicated above, the parameter Îµ0 is a measurement-system constant. Its presence in the equations now used to define electromagnetic quantities is the result of the so-called "rationalization" process described below. But the method of allocating a value to it is a consequence of the result that Maxwell's equations predict that, in free space, electromagnetic waves move with the speed of light. Understanding why Îµ0 has the value it does requires a brief understanding of the history.
