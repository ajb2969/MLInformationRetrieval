   Bramble–Hilbert lemma      Bramble–Hilbert lemma  In [[mathematics]], particularly [[numerical analysis]], the '''Bramble–Hilbert [[lemma (mathematics)|lemma]]''', named after [[James H. Bramble]] and [[Stephen Hilbert]], bounds the [[approxima tion error|error]] of an approximation of a function    u   u   \textstyle u   by a polynomial of order at most    m  -  1      m  1    \textstyle m-1   in terms of derivatives of   u   u   \textstyle u   of order   m   m   \textstyle m   . Both the error of the approximation and the derivatives of   u   u   \textstyle u   are measured by     L  p     superscript  L  p    \textstyle L^{p}   norms on a bounded  domain in    ℝ  n     superscript  ℝ  n    \textstyle\mathbb{R}^{n}   . This is similar to classical numerical analysis, where, for example, the error of linear interpolation    u   u   \textstyle u   can be bounded using the second derivative of   u   u   \textstyle u   . However, the Bramble–Hilbert lemma applies in any number of dimensions, not just one dimension, and the approximation error and the derivatives of   u   u   \textstyle u   are measured by more general norms involving averages, not just the maximum norm .  Additional assumptions on the domain are needed for the Bramble–Hilbert lemma to hold. Essentially, the boundary of the domain must be "reasonable". For example, domains that have a spike or a slit with zero angle at the tip are excluded. Lipschitz domains are reasonable enough, which includes convex domains and domains with continuously differentiable boundary.  The main use of the Bramble–Hilbert lemma is to prove bounds on the error of interpolation of function   u   u   \textstyle u   by an operator that preserves polynomials of order up to    m  -  1      m  1    \textstyle m-1   , in terms of the derivatives of   u   u   \textstyle u   of order   m   m   \textstyle m   . This is an essential step in error estimates for the finite element method . The Bramble–Hilbert lemma is applied there on the domain consisting of one element (or, in some superconvergence results, a small number of elements).
