Chordal problem

In the book1 there is a generalization of the equichordal point problem attributed to R. Gardner.

We consider a point O O O inside a Jordan curve with the property that for any chord [ X , Y ] X Y [X,Y] of the curve passing through O O O the two parts [ X , O ] X O [X,O] and [ O , Y ] O Y [O,Y] of the chord satisfy the following equation, where α α \alpha is a fixed real number:

The template for all chordal problems is this:

Problem: Is there a curve with two or more distinct points with this property?

The status of various special cases

=== The equichordal point problem (α = 1) === This has been the most famous of the chordal problems. In this case, the equation12 states that every chord passing through O O O has the same length. It has become known as the equichordal point problem, and was fully solved in 1996 by Marek Rychlik.

=== The equireciprocal point problem (α = −1)===

Klee13 proved that the ellipse solves the equireciprocal point problem, with the ellipse foci serving as the two equireciprocal points. However, in addition to the ellipses, many solutions of low smoothness also exist, as it was shown in.14 From the point of view of the equichordal point problem, this is due to the lack of hyperbolicity of the fixed points of a certain map of the plane.

Other cases

The method used in Rychlik's proof for the equichordal point problem may only generalize to some rational values of α α \alpha . A reasonable conjecture could be:

Conjecture: There are no solutions of the Chordal Problem for rational α α \alpha close to 1.
