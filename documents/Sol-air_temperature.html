Sol-air temperature

Sol-air temperature (Tsol-air) is a variable used to calculate cooling load of a building and determine the total heat gain through exterior surfaces. It is an improvement over:

q A = h o ( T o - T s ) q A subscript h o subscript T o subscript T s \frac{q}{A}=h_{o}(T_{o}-T_{s})

Where:

  • q q q = rate of heat transfer [W]
  • A A A = heat transfer surface area [m²]
  • h o subscript h o h_{o} = heat transfer coefficient for radiation (long wave) and convection [W/m²K]
  • T o subscript T o T_{o} = outdoor surroundings' temperature [°C]
  • T s subscript T s T_{s} = outside surface temperature [°C]

The above equation only takes into account the temperature differences and ignores two important parameters, being 1) solar radiative flux; and 2) infrared exchanges from the sky. The concept of Tsol-air was thus introduced to enable these parameters to be included within an improved calculation. The lower formula results:

  • a a a = solar radiation absortivity (surface solar absorptance or the inverse of the solar reflectance of a material) [-]
  • I I I = global solar irradiance (i.e. total solar radiation incident on the surface) [W/m²]
  • Δ Q i r normal-Δ subscript Q i r \Delta Q_{ir} = extra infrared radiation due to difference between the external air temperature and the apparent sky temperature. The above equation is used for opaque facades in,1 and renders intermediate calculation of T sol - air subscript T sol air T_{\mathrm{sol-air}} unnecessary. The main advantage of this latter approach is that it avoids the need for a different outdoor temperature node for each facade. Thus, the solution scheme is kept simple, and the solar and sky radiation terms from all facades can be aggregated and distributed to internal temperature nodes as gains/losses.
