In probability theory and statistics, the noncentral chi-squared or noncentral χ 2 superscript χ 2 \chi^{2} distribution is a generalization of the chi-squared distribution. This distribution often arises in the power analysis of statistical tests in which the null distribution is (perhaps asymptotically) a chi-squared distribution; important examples of such tests are the likelihood ratio tests.

Background

Let ( X 1 subscript X 1 X_{1} , X 2 , … , subscript X 2 normal-… X_{2},\ldots, X i , … , subscript X i normal-… X_{i},\ldots, X k subscript X k X_{k} ) be k independent, normally distributed random variables with means μ i subscript μ i \mu_{i} and unit variances. Then the random variable

is distributed according to the noncentral chi-squared distribution. It has two parameters k k k which specifies the number of degrees of freedom (i.e. the number of X i subscript X i X_{i} ), and λ λ \lambda which is related to the mean of the random variables X i subscript X i X_{i} by:

λ = ∑ i = 1 k μ i 2 . λ superscript subscript i 1 k superscript subscript μ i 2 \lambda=\sum_{i=1}^{k}\mu_{i}^{2}. λ λ \lambda is sometimes called the noncentrality parameter. Note that some references define λ λ \lambda in other ways, such as half of the above sum, or its square root.

This distribution arises in multivariate statistics as a derivative of the multivariate normal distribution. While the central chi-squared distribution is the squared norm of a random vector with N ( 0 k , I k ) N subscript 0 k subscript I k N(0_{k},I_{k}) distribution (i.e., the squared distance from the origin of a point taken at random from that distribution), the non-central χ 2 superscript χ 2 \chi^{2} is the squared norm of a random vector with N ( μ , I k ) N μ subscript I k N(\mu,I_{k}) distribution. Here 0 k subscript 0 k 0_{k} is a zero vector of length k, μ = ( μ 1 , … , μ k ) μ subscript μ 1 normal-… subscript μ k \mu=(\mu_{1},\ldots,\mu_{k}) and I k subscript I k I_{k} is the identity matrix of size k.

Use in tolerance intervals

Two-sided normal regression tolerance intervals can be obtained based on the noncentral chi-squared distribution.7 This enables the calculation of a statistical interval within which, with some confidence level, a specified proportion of a sampled population falls.
