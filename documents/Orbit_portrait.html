Orbit portrait

In mathematics, an orbit portrait is a combinatorial tool used in complex dynamics for understanding the behavior of one-complex dimensional quadratic maps.

In simple words one can say that it is :

  • a list of external angles for which rays land on points of that orbit

Formal orbit portraits

Every orbit portrait 𝒫 𝒫 {\mathcal{P}} has the following properties:

  • Each A j subscript A j A_{j} is a finite subset of ℝ / ℤ ℝ ℤ {\mathbb{R}}/{\mathbb{Z}}
  • The doubling map on the circle gives a bijection from A j subscript A j A_{j} to A j + 1 subscript A j 1 A_{j+1} and preserves cyclic order of the angles.3
  • All of the angles in all of the sets A 1 , … , A n subscript A 1 normal-… subscript A n A_{1},\ldots,A_{n} are periodic under the doubling map of the circle, and all of the angles have the same exact period. This period must be a multiple of n n n , so the period is of the form r n r n rn , where r r r is called the recurrent ray period.
  • The sets A j subscript A j A_{j} are pairwise unlinked, which is to say that given any pair of them, there are two disjoint intervals of ℝ / ℤ ℝ ℤ {\mathbb{R}}/{\mathbb{Z}} where each interval contains one of the sets.

Any collection { A 1 , … , A n } subscript A 1 normal-… subscript A n \{A_{1},\ldots,A_{n}\} of subsets of the circle which satisfy these four properties above is called a formal orbit portrait. It is a theorem of John Milnor that every formal orbit portrait is realized by the actual orbit portrait of a periodic orbit of some quadratic one-complex-dimensional map. Orbit portraits contain dynamical information about how external rays and their landing points map in the plane, but formal orbit portraits are no more than combinatorial objects. Milnor's theorem states that, in truth, there is no distinction between the two.

Sectors

Much as the rays landing on the orbit divide up the circle, they divide up the complex plane. For every point z j subscript z j z_{j} of the orbit, the external rays landing at z j subscript z j z_{j} divide the plane into v v v open sets called sectors based at z j subscript z j z_{j} . Sectors are naturally identified the complementary arcs based at the same point. The angular width of a sector is defined as the length of its corresponding complementary arc. Sectors are called critical sectors or critical value sectors when the corresponding arcs are, respectively, critical arcs and critical value arcs.4

Sectors also have the interesting property that 0 0 is in the critical sector of every point, and c c c , the critical value of f c subscript f c f_{c} , is in the critical value sector.

Generalizations

Orbit portraits turn out to be useful combinatorial objects in studying the connection between the dynamics and the parameter spaces of other families of maps as well. In particular, they have been used to study the patterns of all periodic dynamical rays landing on a periodic cycle of a unicritical anti-holomorphic polynomial.5
