Papyrus 49

Papyrus 49 (Gregory-Aland), designated by ğ”“ ğ”“ \mathfrak{P} 49, is an early copy of the New Testament in Greek. It is a papyrus manuscript of the Epistle to the Ephesians, surviving in a fragmentary condition. The manuscript has been palaeographically assigned to the 3rd century. It was probably a part of the same manuscript as Papyrus 65. It came from Egypt and was purchased for the Yale University Library. Textually it is close to the Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. The text of the manuscript has been published several times.

Description

The original size of the leaf was 18 centimetres in height by 25 centimetres in width.1 The leaf is damaged at the top, and six lines of its text have been lost. At the present time the leaf measures 20.3Â cm by 13.3Â cm. The lower and outer margins are 3 centimetres wide; the upper and inner margins were lost.2

The manuscript has survived in a fragmentary condition and contains the texts of Ephesians 4:16-29; 4:31-5:13.34 According to Kurt Aland, it is one of three early manuscripts with the text of the Epistle to the Ephesians.56

The text is written in one column per page of 29 lines, with 38 letters per line (average).7 It has no breathings (spiritus asper, spiritus lenis) nor accents. The double point (:) is the only mark of punctuation. The letters are slightly inclined to the right; the writing shows the influence of cursive handwriting.8 It was written by a professional scribe.9 The nomina sacra are written in an abbreviated way.10

The Greek text of this codex is a representative of the Alexandrian text-type. Aland ascribed it as â€œat least Normal textâ€, and placed it in Category I.11 Bruce M. Metzger identifies it as Alexandrian text. According to Philip Comfort and David Barrett the manuscript shows a strong agreement with Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus (in 14 out of 16 textual variants).12

According to Comfort, ğ”“ ğ”“ \mathfrak{P} 49 and ğ”“ ğ”“ \mathfrak{P} 65 came from the same manuscript.13 Both manuscripts have the same measurements, and the same number of lines per page. Both were written by a documentary hand, both manifest the same formation of certain letters, such as titled lambda, titled sigma, extended iota, and toiled ypsilon. The nomina sacra and abbreviations are written in the same rather unusual way.14 Bradford Welles remarks that "there is not a single case of difference in the letter shapes in the two papyri".15 Neither use ligatures.16 The only difference is in the number of letters per line ğ”“ ğ”“ \mathfrak{P} 65 has more letters per line (42).17 The same situation exists in Papyrus 75. The scribe of ğ”“ ğ”“ \mathfrak{P} 75 appears to have realized that he was running out of room in his codex and added three letters in each line and even added extra lines.18 The manuscript may be one of the earliest manuscripts to display a complete Pauline codex, along with Papyrus 13, Papyrus 15/Papyrus 16, Papyrus 30, Papyrus 46, and Papyrus 92.19

History

The handwriting of the manuscript displays many third-century features. Comfort dated the manuscript to the middle of the third century.25 It is presently assigned to the third century on palaeographic grounds by the Institute for New Testament Textual Research (INTF).2627 It was probably written in Egypt, but it cannot be assigned to any particular locality.28

The manuscript was purchased in Cairo for Yale University in February 1931. Its provenance prior to Cairo is not known.29 Currently it is housed at the Yale University Library (P. Yale 415) in New Haven.30

The text of the codex was published by William Hatch and Bradford Welles in 1958 (editio princeps).31 Kurt Aland catalogued the manuscript on the list of the New Testament papyri under the number 49.32 Susan Stephens gave a new and complete transcription of the codex in 1985.33 Comfort and Barrett again gave a new reconstruction in 1999.34

Vittorio Bartoletti (1920â€“1990) was the first to note that Papyrus 49 and Papyrus 65 were produced by the same scribe.35 This conclusion was confirmed by Bradford Welles, Comfort and Barrett, and other palaeographers.36 They are still catalogued as separate manuscripts on the INTF's list.3738
