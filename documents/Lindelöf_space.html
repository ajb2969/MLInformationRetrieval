Lindel√∂f space

In mathematics, a Lindel√∂f space is a topological space in which every open cover has a countable subcover. The Lindel√∂f property is a weakening of the more commonly used notion of compactness, which requires the existence of a finite subcover.

A strongly Lindel√∂f space is a topological space such that every open subspace is Lindel√∂f. Such spaces are also known as hereditarily Lindel√∂f spaces, because all subspaces of such a space are Lindel√∂f.

Lindel√∂f spaces are named for the Finnish mathematician Ernst Leonard Lindel√∂f.

Properties of Lindel√∂f spaces

In general, no implications hold (in either direction) between the Lindel√∂f property and other compactness properties, such as paracompactness. But by the Morita theorem, every regular Lindel√∂f space is paracompact.

Any second-countable space is a Lindel√∂f space, but not conversely. However, the matter is simpler for metric spaces. A metric space is Lindel√∂f if and only if it is separable, and if and only if it is second-countable.

An open subspace of a Lindel√∂f space is not necessarily Lindel√∂f. However, a closed subspace must be Lindel√∂f.

Lindel√∂f is preserved by continuous maps. However, it is not necessarily preserved by products, not even by finite products.

A Lindel√∂f space is compact if and only if it is countably compact.

Properties of strongly Lindel√∂f spaces

  • Any second-countable space is a strongly Lindel√∂f space
  • Any Suslin space is strongly Lindel√∂f.
  • Strongly Lindel√∂f spaces are closed under taking countable unions, subspaces, and continuous images.
  • Every Radon measure on a strongly Lindel√∂f space is moderated.
