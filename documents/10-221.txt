There are different normalization schemes for spheroidal functions. A table of the different schemes can be found in Abramowitz and Stegun. 7 Abramowitz and Stegun (and the present article) follow the notation of Flammer. 8  Originally, the spheroidal wave functions were introduced by C. Niven, 9 which lead to a Helmholtz equation in spheroidal coordinates. Monographs tying together many aspects of the theory of spheroidal wave functions were written by Strutt, 10 Stratton et al., 11 Meixner and Schafke, 12 and Flammer. 13  Flammer 14 provided a thorough discussion of the calculation of the eigenvalues, angular wavefunctions, and radial wavefunctions for both the prolate and the oblate case. Computer programs for this purpose have been developed by many, including King et al., 15 Patz and Van Buren, 16 Baier et al., 17 Zhang and Jin, 18 Thompson, 19 and Falloon. 20 Van Buren and Boisvert 21 22 have recently developed new methods for calculating prolate spheroidal wave functions that extend the ability to obtain numerical values to extremely wide parameter ranges. Fortran source code that combines the new results with traditional methods is available at http://www.mathieuandspheroidalwavefunctions.com .  Tables of numerical values of spheroidal wave functions are given in Flammer, 23 Hunter, 24 25 Hanish et al., 26 27 28 and Van Buren et al. 29  The Digital Library of Mathematical Functions http://dlmf.nist.gov provided by NIST is an excellent resource for spheroidal wave functions.  Prolate spheroidal wave functions whose domain is a (portion of) the surface of the unit sphere are more generally called "Slepian functions" 30 (see also Spectral concentration problem ).
