   Barycentric coordinate system      Barycentric coordinate system   In geometry , the barycentric coordinate system is a coordinate system in which the location of a point of a simplex (a triangle, tetrahedron , etc.) is specified as the center of mass , or barycenter, of usually unequal masses placed at its vertices . Coordinates also extend outside the simplex, where one or more coordinates become negative . The system was introduced (1827) by August Ferdinand Möbius . Barycentric coordinates on triangles  In the context of a triangle , barycentric coordinates are also known as area coordinates or areal coordinates , because the coordinates of P with respect to triangle ABC are equivalent to the (signed) ratios of the areas of PBC , PCA and PAB to the area of the reference triangle ABC . Areal and trilinear coordinates are used for similar purposes in geometry.  Barycentric or areal coordinates are extremely useful in engineering applications involving triangular subdomains . These make analytic integrals often easier to evaluate, and Gaussian quadrature tables are often presented in terms of area coordinates. Generalized barycentric coordinates  Barycentric coordinates ( a 1 , ..., a n ) that are defined with respect to a polytope instead of a simplex are called generalized barycentric coordinates . For these, the equation        (    a  1   +  ⋯  +   a  n    )   p   =     a  1    x  1    +  ⋯  +    a  n    x  n              subscript  a  1   normal-⋯   subscript  a  n    p        subscript  a  1    subscript  x  1    normal-⋯     subscript  a  n    subscript  x  n       (a_{1}+\cdots+a_{n})p=a_{1}x_{1}+\cdots+a_{n}x_{n}     is still required to hold where x 1 , ..., x n are the vertices of the given polytope. Thus, the definition is formally unchanged but while a simplex with n vertices needs to be embedded in a vector space of dimension of at least n-1 , a polytope may be embedded in a vector space of lower dimension. The simplest example is a quadrilateral in the plane. Consequently, even normalized generalized barycentric coordinates (i.e. coordinates such that the sum of the coefficients is 1) are in general not uniquely determined anymore while this is the case for normalized barycentric coordinates with respect to a simplex.  More abstractly, generalized barycentric coordinates express a polytope with n vertices, regardless of dimension, as the image of the standard    (   n  -  1   )      n  1    (n-1)   -simplex, which has n vertices – the map is onto      Δ   n  -  1    ↠  P   .     normal-↠   superscript  normal-Δ    n  1    P    \Delta^{n-1}\twoheadrightarrow P.   The map is one-to-one if and only if the polytope is a simplex, in which case the map is an isomorphism; this corresponds to a point not having unique generalized barycentric coordinates except when P is a simplex. Further, the n -orthant is the standard object that maps to cones.  Applications  Generalized barycentric coordinates have applications in computer graphics and more specifically in geometric modelling . Often, a three-dimensional model can be approximated by a polyhedron such that the generalized barycentric coordinates with respect to that polyhedron have a geometric meaning. In this way, the processing of the model can be simplified by using these meaningful coordinates.
