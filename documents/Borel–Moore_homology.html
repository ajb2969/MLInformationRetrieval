For reasonable compact spaces, Borel‚àíMoore homology coincides with the usual singular homology. For non-compact spaces, each theory has its own advantages. Definition via compactifications

Suppose that X is homeomorphic to the complement of a closed subcomplex S in a finite CW complex Y. Then Borel‚ÄìMoore homology HiBM(X) is isomorphic to the relative homology Hi(Y, S). Under the same assumption on X, the one-point compactification of X is homeomorphic to a finite CW complex. As a result, Borel‚ÄìMoore homology can be viewed as the relative homology of the one-point compactification with respect to the added point.

Example

Let X = R2 ‚àí 0, the plane minus the origin. The usual homology of X is Z in degrees 0 and 1 (and zero otherwise), while the Borel‚àíMoore homology of X is Z in degrees 1 and 2 (and zero otherwise). In this example, the natural homomorphism Hi(X) ‚Üí HiBM(X) is zero for all integers i. For example, the usual homology H1(X) is generated by a circle around the origin, but this maps to zero in Borel‚ÄìMoore homology, because a finite 1-chain representing the circle is the boundary of a locally finite 2-chain whose image is the region inside the circle. The Borel‚ÄìMoore homology H1BM(X) is generated by a locally finite 1-chain whose image is (for example) the ray from 0 to ‚àû in the real line R inside R2.
