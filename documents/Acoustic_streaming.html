Acoustic streaming

Acoustic streaming is a steady current in a fluid driven by the absorption of high amplitude acoustic oscillations. This phenomenon can be observed near sound emitters, or in the standing waves within a Kundt's tube. It is the less-known opposite of sound generation by a flow.

There are two situations where sound is absorbed in its medium of propagation:

  • during propagation.1 The attenuation coefficient is α = 2 η ω 2 / ( 3 ρ c 3 ) α 2 η superscript ω 2 3 ρ superscript c 3 \alpha=2\eta\omega^{2}/(3\rho c^{3}) , following Stokes' law (sound attenuation). This effect is more intense at elevated frequencies and is much greater in air (where attenuation occurs on a characteristic distance α - 1 superscript α 1 \alpha^{-1} ~10 cm at 1 MHz) than in water ( α - 1 superscript α 1 \alpha^{-1} ~100 m at 1 MHz). In air it is known as the Quartz wind.
  • near a boundary. Either when sound reaches a boundary, or when a boundary is vibrating in a still medium.2 A wall vibrating parallel to itself generates a shear wave, of attenuated amplitude within the Stokes oscillating boundary layer. This effect is localised on an attenuation length of characteristic size δ = [ η / ( ρ ω ) ] 1 / 2 δ superscript delimited-[] η ρ ω 1 2 \delta=[\eta/(\rho\omega)]^{1/2} whose order of magnitude is a few micrometres in both air and water at 1 MHz.
