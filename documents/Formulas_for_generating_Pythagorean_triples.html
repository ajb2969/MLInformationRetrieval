Formulas for generating Pythagorean triples

Besides Euclid's formula, many other formulas for generating Pythagorean triples have been developed.

Fibonacci's method

Leonardo of Pisa (c. 1170 – c. 1250) described this method12 for generating primitive triples using the sequence of consecutive odd integers 1 , 3 , 5 , 7 , 9 , 11 , … 1 3 5 7 9 11 normal-… 1,3,5,7,9,11,\ldots , and the fact that the sum of the first n n n terms of this sequence is n 2 superscript n 2 n^{2} . If k k k is the n n n -th member of this sequence then n = ( k + 1 ) / 2 n k 1 2 n=(k+1)/2 .

Choose any odd square number k k k from this sequence ( k = a 2 k superscript a 2 k=a^{2} ) and let this square be the n n n -th term of the sequence. Also, let b 2 superscript b 2 b^{2} be the sum of the previous n - 1 n 1 n-1 terms, and let c 2 superscript c 2 c^{2} be the sum of all n n n terms . Then we have established that a 2 + b 2 = c 2 superscript a 2 superscript b 2 superscript c 2 a^{2}+b^{2}=c^{2} and we have generated the primitive triple [ a, b, c]. This method produces an infinite number of primitive triples, but not all of them.

EXAMPLE: Choose k = 9 = 3 2 = a 2 k 9 superscript 3 2 superscript a 2 k=9=3^{2}=a^{2} . This odd square number is the fifth term of the sequence, because 5 = n = ( a 2 + 1 ) / 2 5 n superscript a 2 1 2 5=n=(a^{2}+1)/2 . The sum of the previous 4 terms is b 2 = 4 2 superscript b 2 superscript 4 2 b^{2}=4^{2} and the sum of all n = 5 n 5 n=5 terms is c 2 = 5 2 superscript c 2 superscript 5 2 c^{2}=5^{2} giving us a 2 + b 2 = c 2 superscript a 2 superscript b 2 superscript c 2 a^{2}+b^{2}=c^{2} and the primitive triple [ a, b, c] = [ 3, 4, 5].

Dickson's method

Leonard Eugene Dickson (1920)5 attributes to himself the following method for generating Pythagorean triples. To find integer solutions to x 2 + y 2 = z 2 superscript x 2 superscript y 2 superscript z 2 x^{2}+y^{2}=z^{2} , find positive integers r, s, and t such that r 2 = 2 s t superscript r 2 2 s t r^{2}=2st is a square.

Then:

x = r + s , y = r + t , z = r + s + t . formulae-sequence x r s formulae-sequence y r t z r s t x=r+s\,,\,y=r+t\,,\,z=r+s+t.

From this we see that r r r is any even integer and that s and t are factors of r 2 2 superscript r 2 2 \tfrac{r^{2}}{2} .  All Pythagorean triples may be found by this method.  When s and t are coprime the triple will be primitive. A simple proof of Dickson's method has been presented by Josef Rukavicka (2013).6

Example: Choose r = 6. Then r 2 2 = 18 superscript r 2 2 18 \tfrac{r^{2}}{2}=18 . The three factor-pairs of 18 are: (1, 18), (2, 9), and (3, 6). All three factor pairs will produce triples using the above equations.

s = 1, t = 18 produces the triple [7, 24, 25] because x = 6 + 1 = 7,  y = 6 + 18 = 24,  z = 6 + 1 + 18 = 25.
s = 2, t =   9 produces the triple [8, 15, 17] because x = 6 + 2 = 8,  y = 6 +  9 = 15,  z = 6 + 2 + 9 = 17.
s = 3, t =   6 produces the triple [9, 12, 15] because x = 6 + 3 = 9,  y = 6 +  6 = 12,  z = 6 + 3 + 6 = 15. (Since s and t are not coprime, this triple is not primitive.)

Height-excess enumeration theorem

Wade and Wade17 first introduced the categorization of Pythagorean triples by their height, defined as c - b, linking 3,4,5 to 5,12,13 and 7,24,25 and so on.

McCullough and Wade18 extended this approach, which produces all Pythagorean triples when k > h 2 d : normal-: k h 2 d absent k>\frac{h\sqrt{2}}{d}: Write a positive integer h as pq2 with p square-free and q positive. Set d = 2pq if p is odd, or d= pq if p is even. For all pairs (h,k) of positive integers, the triples are given by
