   Candela      Candela   (Figure)  Linear visible spectrum.svg   The candela ( or ; symbol: cd ) is the SI base unit of luminous intensity ; that is, luminous power per unit solid angle emitted by a point light source in a particular direction. Luminous intensity is analogous to radiant intensity , but instead of simply adding up the contributions of every wavelength of light in the source's spectrum, the contribution of each wavelength is weighted by the standard luminosity function (a model of the sensitivity of the human eye to different wavelengths). 1 2 A common candle emits light with a luminous intensity of roughly one candela. If emission in some directions is blocked by an opaque barrier, the emission would still be approximately one candela in the directions that are not obscured.  The word candela means candle in Latin . Origin  Prior to 1948, various standards for luminous intensity were in use in a number of countries. These were typically based on the brightness of the flame from a "standard candle" of defined composition, or the brightness of an incandescent filament of specific design. One of the best-known of these was the English standard of candlepower. One candlepower was the light produced by a pure spermaceti candle weighing one sixth of a pound and burning at a rate of 120 grains per hour. Germany, Austria and Scandinavia used the Hefnerkerze , a unit based on the output of a Hefner lamp . 5  It became clear that a better-defined unit was needed. The Commission Internationale de l'Ã‰clairage (International Commission on Illumination) and the CIPM proposed a â€œnew candleâ€ based on the luminance of a Planck radiator (a black body ) at the temperature of freezing platinum . The value of the new unit was chosen to make it similar to the earlier unit candlepower. The decision was promulgated by the CIPM in 1946:   The value of the new candle is such that the brightness of the full radiator at the temperature of solidification of platinum is 60Â new candles per square centimetre . 6   It was then ratified in 1948 by the 9thÂ CGPM which adopted a new name for this unit, the candela . In 1967 the 13th CGPM removed the term "new candle" and gave an amended version of the candela definition, specifying the atmospheric pressure applied to the freezing platinum:   The candela is the luminous intensity, in the perpendicular direction, of a surface of  squareÂ metre of a black body at the temperature of freezing platinum under a pressure of Â newtons per squareÂ metre. 7   In 1979, because of the difficulties in realizing a Planck radiator at high temperatures and the new possibilities offered by radiometry , the 16thÂ CGPM adopted the modern definition of the candela. 8 The arbitrary (1/683) term was chosen so that the new definition would exactly match the old definition.
