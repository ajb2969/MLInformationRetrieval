Tautology (rule of inference)

In propositional logic, tautology is one of two commonly used rules of replacement.123 The rules are used to eliminate redundancy in disjunctions and conjunctions when they occur in logical proofs. They are:

The principle of idempotency of disjunction:

P P ⇔ P normal-⇔ P P P PP\Leftrightarrow P

and the principle of idempotency of conjunction:

P and P ⇔ P normal-⇔ P italic- and P P P\and P\Leftrightarrow P

Where " ⇔ normal-⇔ \Leftrightarrow " is a metalogical symbol representing "can be replaced in a logical proof with."

Relation to tautology

The rule gets its name from the fact that the concept of the rule is the same as the tautologous statements If "p and p" is true then "p" is true. and If "p or p" is true then "p" is true. This type of tautology is called idempotency. Although the rule is the expression of a particular tautology, this is a bit misleading, as every rule of inference can be expressed as a tautology and vice versa.

Formal notation

Theorems are those logical formulas ϕ ϕ \phi where ⊢ ϕ proves absent ϕ \vdash\phi is the conclusion of a valid proof,4 while the equivalent semantic consequence ⊧ ϕ models absent ϕ \models\phi indicates a tautology.

The tautology rule may be expressed as a sequent:

P P ⊢ P proves P P P PP\vdash P\,

and

P and P ⊢ P proves P italic- and P P P\and P\vdash P\,

where ⊢ proves \vdash is a metalogical symbol meaning that P P P is a syntactic consequence of P P P P PP , in the one case, P and P P italic- and P P\and P in the other, in some logical system;

or as a rule of inference:

P P ∴ P P P therefore absent P \frac{PP}{\therefore P}

and

P and P ∴ P P italic- and P therefore absent P \frac{P\and P}{\therefore P}

where the rule is that wherever an instance of " P P P P PP " or " P and P P italic- and P P\and P " appears on a line of a proof, it can be replaced with " P P P ";

or as the statement of a truth-functional tautology or theorem of propositional logic. The principle was stated as a theorem of propositional logic by Russell and Whitehead in Principia Mathematica as:

( P P ) → P normal-→ P P P (PP)\to P\,

and

( P and P ) → P normal-→ P italic- and P P (P\and P)\to P\,

where P P P is a proposition expressed in some formal system.
