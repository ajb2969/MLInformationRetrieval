Motivation

By giving a collection of subspaces of some vector space a topological structure, it is possible to talk about a continuous choice of subspace or open and closed collections of subspaces; by giving them the structure of a differential manifold one can talk about smooth choices of subspace.

A natural example comes from tangent bundles of smooth manifolds embedded in Euclidean space. Suppose we have a manifold M M M of dimension r r r embedded in . At each point x x x in M M M , the tangent space to M M M can be considered as a subspace of the tangent space of , which is just . The map assigning to x x x its tangent space defines a map from M M M to ğ†ğ« ( r , n ) ğ†ğ« r n \mathbf{Gr}(r,n) . (In order to do this, we have to translate the geometrical tangent space to M M M so that it passes through the origin rather than x x x , and hence defines a r r r -dimensional vector subspace. This idea is very similar to the Gauss map for surfaces in a 3-dimensional space.)

This idea can with some effort be extended to all vector bundles over a manifold M M M , so that every vector bundle generates a continuous map from M M M to a suitably generalised Grassmannianâ€”although various embedding theorems must be proved to show this. We then find that the properties of our vector bundles are related to the properties of the corresponding maps viewed as continuous maps. In particular we find that vector bundles inducing homotopic maps to the Grassmannian are isomorphic. But the definition of homotopic relies on a notion of continuity, and hence a topology.

Applications

Grassmann manifolds have found application in computer vision tasks of video-based face recognition and shape recognition.4

Grassmannians allow the scattering amplitudes of subatomic particles to be calculated via a positive Grassmannian construct called the amplituhedron.5
