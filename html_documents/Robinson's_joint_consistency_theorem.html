Robinson's joint consistency theorem

Robinson's joint consistency theorem is an important theorem of mathematical logic. It is related to Craig interpolation and Beth definability.

The classical formulation of Robinson's joint consistency theorem is as follows:

Let T 1 subscript T 1 T_{1} and T 2 subscript T 2 T_{2} be first-order theories. If T 1 subscript T 1 T_{1} and T 2 subscript T 2 T_{2} are consistent and the intersection T 1 ∩ T 2 subscript T 1 subscript T 2 T_{1}\cap T_{2} is complete (in the common language of T 1 subscript T 1 T_{1} and T 2 subscript T 2 T_{2} ), then the union T 1 ∪ T 2 subscript T 1 subscript T 2 T_{1}\cup T_{2} is consistent. Note that a theory is complete if it decides every formula, i.e. either T ⊢ φ proves T φ T\vdash\varphi or T ⊢ ¬ φ proves T φ T\vdash\neg\varphi .

Since the completeness assumption is quite hard to fulfill, there is a variant of the theorem:

Let T 1 subscript T 1 T_{1} and T 2 subscript T 2 T_{2} be first-order theories. If T 1 subscript T 1 T_{1} and T 2 subscript T 2 T_{2} are consistent and if there is no formula φ φ \varphi in the common language of T 1 subscript T 1 T_{1} and T 2 subscript T 2 T_{2} such that T 1 ⊢ φ proves subscript T 1 φ T_{1}\vdash\varphi and T 2 ⊢ ¬ φ proves subscript T 2 φ T_{2}\vdash\neg\varphi , then the union T 1 ∪ T 2 subscript T 1 subscript T 2 T_{1}\cup T_{2} is consistent.
