In probability theory, a logit-normal distribution is a probability distribution of a random variable whose logit has a normal distribution. If Y is a random variable with a normal distribution, and P is the logistic function, then XÂ =Â P(Y) has a logit-normal distribution; likewise, if X is logit-normally distributed, then YÂ =Â logit(X)= log (X/(1-X)) is normally distributed. It is also known as the logistic normal distribution,1 which often refers to a multinomial logit version (e.g.2345).

A variable might be modeled as logit-normal if it is a proportion, which is bounded by zero and one, and where values of zero and one never occur.

Use in statistical analysis

The logistic normal distribution is a more flexible alternative to the Dirichlet distribution in that it can capture correlations between components of probability vectors. It also has the potential to simplify statistical analyses of compositional data by allowing one to answer questions about log-ratios of the components of the data vectors. One is often interested in ratios rather than absolute component values.

The probability simplex is a bounded space, making standard techniques that are typically applied to vectors in â„ n superscript â„ n \mathbb{R}^{n} less meaningful. Aitchison described the problem of spurious negative correlations when applying such methods directly to simplicial vectors.9 However, mapping compositional data in ğ’® D superscript ğ’® D \mathcal{S}^{D} through the inverse of the additive logistic transformation yields real-valued data in â„ D - 1 superscript â„ D 1 \mathbb{R}^{D-1} . Standard techniques can be applied to this representation of the data. This approach justifies use of the logistic normal distribution, which can thus be regarded as the "Gaussian of the simplex".
