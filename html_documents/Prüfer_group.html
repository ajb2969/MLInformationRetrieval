This sequence of inclusions expresses the PrÃ¼fer p-group as the direct limit of its finite subgroups. As there is no maximal subgroup of a PrÃ¼fer p-group, it is its own Frattini subgroup.

Given this list of subgroups, it is clear that the PrÃ¼fer p-groups are indecomposable (cannot be written as a direct sum of proper subgroups). More is true: the PrÃ¼fer p-groups are subdirectly irreducible. An abelian group is subdirectly irreducible if and only if it is isomorphic to a finite cyclic p-group or to a PrÃ¼fer group.

The PrÃ¼fer p-group is the unique infinite p-group which is locally cyclic (every finite set of elements generates a cyclic group). As seen above, all proper subgroups of Z(pâˆ) are finite. The PrÃ¼fer p-groups are the only infinite abelian groups with this property.1

The PrÃ¼fer p-groups are divisible. They play an important role in the classification of divisible groups; along with the rational numbers they are the simplest divisible groups. More precisely: an abelian group is divisible if and only if it is the direct sum of a (possibly infinite) number of copies of Q and (possibly infinite) numbers of copies of Z(pâˆ) for every prime p. The numbers of copies of Q and Z(pâˆ) that are used in this direct sum determine the divisible group up to isomorphism.2

As an abelian group (that is, as a Z-module), Z(pâˆ) is Artinian but not Noetherian.3 It can thus be used as a counterexample against the idea that every Artinian module is Noetherian (whereas every Artinian ring is Noetherian).

The endomorphism ring of Z(pâˆ) is isomorphic to the ring of p-adic integers Zp.4

In the theory of locally compact topological groups the PrÃ¼fer p-group (endowed with the discrete topology) is the Pontryagin dual of the compact group of p-adic integers, and the group of p-adic integers is the Pontryagin dual of the PrÃ¼fer p-group.5
