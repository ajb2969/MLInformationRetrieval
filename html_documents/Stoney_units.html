Stoney units

In physics the Stoney units form a system of units named after the Irish physicist George Johnstone Stoney, who first proposed them in 1881. They are the first historical example of natural units, i.e. units of measurement designed so that certain fundamental physical constants serve as base units. The set of constants that Stoney used as base units is the following: 12

  • c, the speed of light in a vacuum,
  • G, the gravitational constant,
  • ke = 1/(4πε0), the Coulomb constant,
  • e, the elementary charge.

This means that, in terms of Stoney units, the numerical values of all these constants equal one:

c = G = k e = e = 1. c G subscript k e e 1. c=G=k_{\text{e}}=e=1.

Stoney's set of base units is similar to the one used in Planck units, proposed independently by Planck thirty years later, but Planck normalized the reduced Planck constant in place of the elementary charge.3 In Stoney units, the numerical value of the reduced Planck constant is not 1, but is

ℏ = 1 α ≈ 137.035999679 , Planck-constant-over-2-pi 1 α 137.035999679 \hbar=\frac{1}{\alpha}\approx 137.035999679,

where α is the fine-structure constant. Planck units are more commonly used than Stoney units in modern physics, especially quantum gravity (including string theory). Rarely, Planck units are referred to as Planck–Stoney units.4

History

George Stoney was one of the first scientists to understand that electric charge was quantized; from this quantization he deduced the units that are now named after him.5 6 James G. O’Hara7 pointed out in 1974 that Stoney’s derived estimate of the unit of charge, 10−20 ampere-second, was 1 / 16 1 16 {1}/{16} of the modern value of the charge of the electron. The reason is that Stoney used the approximated value of 1018 for the number of molecules presented in one cubic millimetre of gas at standard temperature and pressure. Using the modern values for Avogadro’s number and for the volume of a gram-molecule (at s.t.p.) of , the modern value is , instead of Stoney's 1018.

Stoney units and Planck units

The Stoney length and the Stoney energy, collectively called the Stoney scale, are not far from the Planck length and the Planck energy, the Planck scale. The Stoney scale and the Planck scale are the length and energy scales at which quantum processes and gravity occur together. At these scales, a unified theory of physics is thus required. The only notable attempt to construct such a theory from the Stoney scale was that of H. Weyl, who associated a gravitational unit of charge with the Stoney length8910 and who appears to have inspired Dirac’s fascination with the large number hypothesis.11 Since then, the Stoney scale has been largely neglected in the development of modern physics, although it is occasionally discussed to this day.1213 The Planck scale is valid for all known interactions, and does not give prominence to the electromagnetic interaction, as the Stoney scale does.
