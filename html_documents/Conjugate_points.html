Conjugate points

In differential geometry, conjugate points are, roughly, points that can almost be joined by a 1-parameter family of geodesics. For example, on a sphere, the north-pole and south-pole are connected by any meridian.

Definition

Suppose p and q are points on a Riemannian manifold, and γ γ \gamma is a geodesic that connects p and q. Then p and q are conjugate points along γ γ \gamma if there exists a non-zero Jacobi field along γ γ \gamma that vanishes at p and q.

Recall that any Jacobi field can be written as the derivative of a geodesic variation (see the article on Jacobi fields). Therefore, if p and q are conjugate along γ γ \gamma , one can construct a family of geodesics that start at p and almost end at q. In particular, if γ s ( t ) subscript γ s t \gamma_{s}(t) is the family of geodesics whose derivative in s at s = 0 s 0 s=0 generates the Jacobi field J, then the end point of the variation, namely γ s ( 1 ) subscript γ s 1 \gamma_{s}(1) , is the point q only up to first order in s. Therefore, if two points are conjugate, it is not necessary that there exist two distinct geodesics joining them.

Examples

  • On the sphere S 2 superscript S 2 S^{2} , antipodal points are conjugate.
  • On ℝ n superscript ℝ n \mathbb{R}^{n} , there are no conjugate points.
  • On Riemannian manifolds with non-positive sectional curvature, there are no conjugate points.
