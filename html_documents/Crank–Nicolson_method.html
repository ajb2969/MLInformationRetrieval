Crank‚ÄìNicolson method

In numerical analysis, the Crank‚ÄìNicolson method is a finite difference method used for numerically solving the heat equation and similar partial differential equations.1 It is a second-order method in time, it is implicit in time and can be written as an implicit Runge‚ÄìKutta method, and it is numerically stable. The method was developed by John Crank and Phyllis Nicolson in the mid 20th century.2

For diffusion equations (and many other equations), it can be shown the Crank‚ÄìNicolson method is unconditionally stable.3 However, the approximate solutions can still contain (decaying) spurious oscillations if the ratio of time step Œî t t t times the thermal diffusivity to the square of space step, Œî x x x 2 2 {}^{2} , is large (typically larger than 1/2 per Von Neumann stability analysis). For this reason, whenever large time steps or high spatial resolution is necessary, the less accurate backward Euler method is often used, which is both stable and immune to oscillations.

Application in financial mathematics

Because a number of other phenomena can be modeled with the heat equation (often called the diffusion equation in financial mathematics), the Crank‚ÄìNicolson method has been applied to those areas as well.4 Particularly, the Black‚ÄìScholes option pricing model's differential equation can be transformed into the heat equation, and thus numerical solutions for option pricing can be obtained with the Crank‚ÄìNicolson method.

The importance of this for finance, is that option pricing problems, when extended beyond the standard assumptions (e.g. incorporating changing dividends), cannot be solved in closed form, but can be solved using this method. Note however, that for non-smooth final conditions (which happen for most financial instruments), the Crank‚ÄìNicolson method is not satisfactory as numerical oscillations are not damped. For vanilla options, this results in oscillation in the gamma value around the strike price. Therefore, special damping initialization steps are necessary (e.g., fully implicit finite difference method).
