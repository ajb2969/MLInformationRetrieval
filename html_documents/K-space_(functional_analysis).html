K-space (functional analysis)

In mathematics, more specifically in functional analysis, a K-space is an F-space V V V such that every extension of F-spaces (or twisted sum) of the form

0 → ℝ → X → V → 0. normal-→ 0 ℝ normal-→ X normal-→ V normal-→ 0. 0\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\rightarrow X\rightarrow V\rightarrow 0.\,\!

is equivalent to the trivial one1

0 → ℝ → ℝ × V → V → 0. normal-→ 0 ℝ normal-→ ℝ V normal-→ V normal-→ 0. 0\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}\times V\rightarrow V\rightarrow 0.\,\!

where ℝ ℝ \mathbb{R} is the real line.

Examples

  • Finite dimensional Banach spaces are K-spaces.
  • The ℓ p superscript normal-ℓ p \ell^{p} spaces for 0 < p < 1 0 p 1 0 are K-spaces.2
  • N. J. Kalton and N. P. Roberts proved that the Banach space ℓ 1 superscript normal-ℓ 1 \ell^{1} is not a K-space.3
