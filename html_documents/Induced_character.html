Induced character

In mathematics, an induced character is the character of the representation V of a finite group G induced from a representation W of a subgroup H ≤ G. More generally, there is also a notion of induction Ind ( f ) Ind f \operatorname{Ind}(f) of a class function f on H given by the formula

Ind ( f ) ( s ) = 1 | H | ∑ t ∈ G , t - 1 s t ∈ H f ( t - 1 s t ) . Ind f s 1 H subscript formulae-sequence t G superscript t 1 s t H f superscript t 1 s t \operatorname{Ind}(f)(s)=\frac{1}{|H|}\sum_{t\in G,\ t^{-1}st\in H}f(t^{-1}st).

If f is a character of the representation W of H, then this formula for Ind ( f ) Ind f \operatorname{Ind}(f) calculates the character of the induced representation V of G.1

The basic result on induced characters is Brauer's theorem on induced characters. It states that every irreducible character on G is a linear combination with integer coefficients of characters induced from elementary subgroups.
