Orthoptic (geometry)

In the geometry of curves, an orthoptic is the set of points for which two tangents of a given curve meet at a right angle.

Examples:

The orthoptic of

1) a parabola is its directrix (proof: s. parabola),
2) an ellipse x 2 a 2 + y 2 b 2 = 1 superscript x 2 superscript a 2 superscript y 2 superscript b 2 1 \frac{x^{2}}{a^{2}}+\frac{y^{2}}{b^{2}}=1 is the director circle x 2 + y 2 = a 2 + b 2 superscript x 2 superscript y 2 superscript a 2 superscript b 2 x^{2}+y^{2}=a^{2}+b^{2} (s. below),
3) a hyperbola x 2 a 2 - y 2 b 2 = 1 , a > b , formulae-sequence superscript x 2 superscript a 2 superscript y 2 superscript b 2 1 a b \frac{x^{2}}{a^{2}}-\frac{y^{2}}{b^{2}}=1,\,a>b, is the circle x 2 + y 2 = a 2 - b 2 superscript x 2 superscript y 2 superscript a 2 superscript b 2 x^{2}+y^{2}=a^{2}-b^{2} (in case of a ≤ b a b a\leq b there are no orthogonal tangents, s. below),

Generalizations:
1) An isoptic is the set of points for which two tangents of a given curve meet at a fixed angle (s. below).
2) An isoptic of two plane curves is the set of points for which two tangents meet at a fixed angle.
3) Thales' theorem on a chord P 1 P 2 ¯ normal-¯ subscript P 1 subscript P 2 \overline{P_{1}P_{2}} can be considered as the orthoptic of two circles which are degenerated to the two points P 1 , P 2 subscript P 1 subscript P 2 P_{1},P_{2} .

Remark: In medicine there exists the term orthoptic, too.
