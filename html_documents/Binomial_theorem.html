History

Special cases of the binomial theorem were known from ancient times. The 4th century B.C. Greek mathematician Euclid mentioned the special case of the binomial theorem for exponent 2.12 There is evidence that the binomial theorem for cubes was known by the 6th century in India.34

Binomial coefficients, as combinatorial quantities expressing the number of ways of selecting k objects out of n without replacement, were of interest to the ancient Hindus. The earliest known reference to this combinatorial problem is the Chandaḥśāstra by the Hindu lyricist Pingala (c. 200 B.C.), which contains a method for its solution.5 The commentator Halayudha from the 10th century A.D. explains this method using what is now known as Pascal's triangle.6 By the 6th century A.D., the Hindu mathematicians probably knew how to express this as a quotient n ! ( n - k ) ! k ! n n k k \frac{n!}{(n-k)!k!} ,7 and a clear statement of this rule can be found in the 12th century text Lilavati by Bhaskara.8

The binomial theorem as such can be found in the work of 11th-century Arabian mathematician Al-Karaji, who described the triangular pattern of the binomial coefficients.9 He also provided a mathematical proof of both the binomial theorem and Pascal's triangle, using a primitive form of mathematical induction.10 The Persian poet and mathematician Omar Khayyam was probably familiar with the formula to higher orders, although many of his mathematical works are lost.11 The binomial expansions of small degrees were known in the 13th century mathematical works of Yang Hui12 and also Chu Shih-Chieh.13 Yang Hui attributes the method to a much earlier 11th century text of Jia Xian, although those writings are now also lost.14

In 1544, Michael Stifel introduced the term "binomial coefficient" and showed how to use them to express ( 1 + a ) n superscript 1 a n (1+a)^{n} in terms of ( 1 + a ) n - 1 superscript 1 a n 1 (1+a)^{n-1} , via "Pascal's triangle".15 Blaise Pascal studied the eponymous triangle comprehensively in the treatise Traité du triangle arithmétique (1653). However, the pattern of numbers was already known to the European mathematicians of the late Renaissance, including Stifel, Niccolò Fontana Tartaglia, and Simon Stevin.16 Isaac Newton is generally credited with the generalised binomial theorem, valid for any rational exponent.1718

The binomial coefficients

The coefficients that appear in the binomial expansion are called binomial coefficients. These are usually written ( n k ) binomial n k {\textstyle\left({{n}\atop{k}}\right)} , and pronounced “n choose k”.

Combinatorial interpretation

The binomial coefficient ( n k ) binomial n k {\textstyle\left({{n}\atop{k}}\right)} can be interpreted as the number of ways to choose k elements from an n-element set. This is related to binomials for the following reason: if we write (x + y)n as a product

( x + y ) ( x + y ) ( x + y ) ⋯ ( x + y ) , x y x y x y normal-⋯ x y (x+y)(x+y)(x+y)\cdots(x+y), then, according to the distributive law, there will be one term in the expansion for each choice of either x or y from each of the binomials of the product. For example, there will only be one term xn, corresponding to choosing x from each binomial. However, there will be several terms of the form xn−2y2, one for each way of choosing exactly two binomials to contribute a y. Therefore, after combining like terms, the coefficient of xn−2y2 will be equal to the number of ways to choose exactly 2 elements from an n-element set.

General case

Generalisations

Newton's generalised binomial theorem

Around 1665, Isaac Newton generalised the formula to allow real exponents other than nonnegative integers. In addition, the formula can be generalised to complex exponents. In this generalisation, the finite sum is replaced by an infinite series. In order to do this, one needs to give meaning to binomial coefficients with an arbitrary upper index, which cannot be done using the above formula with factorials; however factoring out (n − k)! from numerator and denominator in that formula, and replacing n by r which now stands for an arbitrary number, one can define

Derivative of the power function

In finding the derivative of the power function, f(x) = xn, by using the definition of derivative, the expansion of (x + h)n is employed.

Nth derivative of a product

To indicate the formula for the derivative of order n of the product of two functions, the formula of the binomial theorem is used symbolically.24

The binomial theorem in abstract algebra

Formula (1) is valid more generally for any elements x and y of a semiring satisfying xy = yx. The theorem is true even more generally: alternativity suffices in place of associativity.

The binomial theorem can be stated by saying that the polynomial sequence { 1, x, x2, x3, ... } is of binomial type.
