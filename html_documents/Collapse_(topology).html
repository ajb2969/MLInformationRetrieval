Collapse (topology)

In topology, a branch of mathematics, collapse is a concept due to J. H. C. Whitehead.1

Definition

Let K K K be an abstract simplicial complex.

Suppose that τ , σ ∈ K τ σ K \tau,\sigma\in K such that the following two conditions are satisfied:

(i) τ ⊂ σ τ σ \tau\subset\sigma , in particular dim τ < dim σ dimension τ dimension σ \dim\tau<\dim\sigma ;

(ii) σ σ \sigma is a maximal face of K and no other maximal face of K contains τ τ \tau ,

then τ τ \tau is called a free face.

A simplicial collapse of K is the removal of all simplices γ γ \gamma such that τ ⊆ γ ⊆ σ τ γ σ \tau\subseteq\gamma\subseteq\sigma , where τ τ \tau is a free face. If additionally we have dim τ = dim σ-1, then this is called an elementary collapse.

A simplicial complex that has a sequence of collapses leading to a point is called collapsible. Every collapsible complex is contractible, but the converse is not true.

This definition can be extended to CW-complexes and is the basis for the concept of simple-homotopy equivalence.2

Examples

  • Complexes that do not have a free face cannot be collapsible. Two such interesting examples are Bing's house with two rooms and Zeeman's dunce hat; they are contractible (homotopy equivalent to a point), but not collapsible.
  • Any n-dimensional PL manifold that is collapsible is in fact piecewise-linearly isomorphic to an n-ball.3
