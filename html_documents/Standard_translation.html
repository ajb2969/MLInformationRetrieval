Standard translation

In modal logic, standard translation is a way of transforming formulas of modal logic into formulas of first-order logic which capture the meaning of the modal formulas. Standard translation is defined inductively on the structure of the formula. In short, atomic formulas are mapped onto unary predicates and the objects in the first-order language are the accessible worlds. The logical connectives from propositional logic remain untouched and the modal operators are transformed into first-order formulas according to their semantics.

The formula □ □ p normal-□ normal-□ p \Box\Box p holds in x x x when for all accessible worlds y y y from x x x and for all accessible worlds z z z from y y y , the predicate P P P is true for z z z . Note that the formula is also true when no such accessible worlds exist. In case x x x has no accessible worlds then R ( x , y ) R x y R(x,y) is false but the whole formula is vacuously true: an implication is also true when the antecedent is false.

Standard translation and modal depth

The modal depth of a formula also becomes apparent in the translation to first-order logic. When the modal depth of a formula is k, then the first-order logic formula contains a 'chain' of k transitions from the starting world x x x . The worlds are 'chained' in the sense that these worlds are visited by going from accessible to accessible world. Informally, the number of transitions in the 'longest chain' of transitions in the first-order formula is the modal depth of the formula.

The modal depth of the formula used in the example above is two. The first-order formula indicates that the transitions from x x x to y y y and from y y y to z z z are needed to verify the validity of the formula. This is also the modal depth of the formula as each modal operator increases the modal depth by one.
