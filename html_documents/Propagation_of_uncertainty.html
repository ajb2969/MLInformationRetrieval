Propagation of uncertainty

For the propagation of uncertainty through time, see Chaos theory#Sensitivity to initial conditions.

In statistics, propagation of uncertainty (or propagation of error) is the effect of variables' uncertainties (or errors) on the uncertainty of a function based on them. When the variables are the values of experimental measurements they have uncertainties due to measurement limitations (e.g., instrument precision) which propagate to the combination of variables in the function.

The uncertainty is usually defined by the absolute error Δ x normal-Δ x Δx . Uncertainties can also be defined by the relative error ( Δ x ) / x normal-Δ x x (Δx)/x , which is usually written as a percentage.

Most commonly, the error on a quantity, Δ x normal-Δ x Δx , is given as the standard deviation, σ σ σ . Standard deviation is the positive square root of variance, . The value of a quantity and its error are often expressed as an interval x ± Δ x x normal-± normal-Δ x x±Δx . If the statistical probability distribution of the variable is known or can be assumed, it is possible to derive confidence limits to describe the region within which the true value of the variable may be found. For example, the 68% confidence limits for a one-dimensional variable belonging to a normal distribution are ± one standard deviation from the value, that is, there is approximately a 68% probability that the true value lies in the region x ± σ x normal-± σ x±σ .

If the variables are correlated, then covariance must be taken into account.

Caveats and warnings

Error estimates for non-linear functions are biased on account of using a truncated series expansion. The extent of this bias depends on the nature of the function. For example, the bias on the error calculated for log x increases as x increases, since the expansion to 1+x is a good approximation only when x is small.

In the special case of the inverse 1 / B 1 B 1/B , where B = N ( 0 , 1 ) B N 0 1 B=N(0,1) , the distribution is a reciprocal normal distribution, and there is no definable variance. For such inverse distributions and for ratio distributions, there can be defined probabilities for intervals, which can be computed either by Monte Carlo simulation or, in some cases, by using the Geary–Hinkley transformation.6 The statistics, mean and variance, of the shifted reciprocal function 1 p - B 1 p B \frac{1}{p-B} for B = N ( μ , σ ) B N μ σ B=N(\mu,\sigma) , however, exist in a principal value sense if the difference between the shift or pole p p p and the mean μ μ \mu is real. The mean of this transformed random variable is then indeed the scaled Dawson's function 2 σ F ( p - μ 2 σ ) 2 σ F p μ 2 σ \frac{\sqrt{2}}{\sigma}F\left(\frac{p-\mu}{\sqrt{2}\sigma}\right) .7 In contrast, if the shift p - μ p μ p-\mu is purely complex, the mean exists and is a scaled Faddeeva function, whose exact expression depends on the sign of the imaginary part, Im ( p - μ ) Im p μ \operatorname{Im}(p-\mu) . In both cases, the variance is a simple function of the mean.8 Therefore, the variance has to be considered in a principal value sense if p - μ p μ p-\mu is real, while it exists if the imaginary part of p - μ p μ p-\mu is non-zero. Note that these means and variances are exact, as they do not recur to linearisation of the ratio. The exact covariance of two ratios with a pair of different poles p 1 subscript p 1 p_{1} and p 2 subscript p 2 p_{2} is similarly available.9 The case of the inverse of a complex normal variable B B B , shifted or not, exhibits different characteristics.10

For highly non-linear functions, there exist five categories of probabilistic approaches for uncertainty propagation;11 see Uncertainty Quantification#Methodologies for forward uncertainty propagation for details.
