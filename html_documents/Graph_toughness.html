Graph toughness

In graph theory, toughness is a measure of the connectivity of a graph. A graph G G G is said to be t t t -tough for a given real number t t t if, for every integer k > 1 k 1 k>1 , G G G cannot be split into k k k different connected components by the removal of fewer than t k t k tk vertices. For instance, a graph is 1 1 1 -tough if the number of components formed by removing a set of vertices is always at most as large as the number of removed vertices. The toughness of a graph is the maximum t t t for which it is t t t -tough; this is a finite number for all graphs except the complete graphs, which by convention have infinite toughness.

Examples

Removing k k k vertices from a path graph can split the remaining graph into as many as k + 1 k 1 k+1 connected components. The maximum ratio of components to removed vertices is achieved by removing one vertex (from the interior of the path) and splitting it into two components. Therefore, paths are 1 2 1 2 \frac{1}{2} -tough. In contrast, removing k k k vertices from a cycle graph leaves at most k k k remaining connected components, and sometimes leaves exactly k k k connected components, so a cycle is 1 1 1 -tough.
