Numbers that fulfill equality in the above definition are known as 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} -perfect numbers.4 That is, the 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} -perfect numbers are the natural numbers that are equal the sum of their divisors in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} . The first few 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} -perfect numbers are:

Examples

Every deficient number and every perfect number is in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} because the restriction of the divisors sum to members of 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} either decreases the divisors sum or leaves it unchanged. The first natural number that is not in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} is the smallest abundant number, which is 12. The next two abundant numbers, 18 and 20, are also not in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} . However, the fourth abundant number, 24, is in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} because the sum of its proper divisors in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} is:

1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 6 + 8 = 24

In other words, 24 is abundant but not 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} -abundant because 12 is not in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} . In fact, 24 is 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} -perfect - it is the smallest number that is 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} -perfect but not perfect.

The smallest odd abundant number that is in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} is 2835, and the smallest pair of consecutive numbers that are not in 𝒮 𝒮 \mathcal{S} are 5984 and 5985.7
