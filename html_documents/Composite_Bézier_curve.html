Composite BÃ©zier curve

In geometric modelling and in computer graphics, a composite BÃ©zier curve is a piecewise BÃ©zier curve that has at least C0 continuity. In other words, a composite BÃ©zier curve is a series of BÃ©zier curves joined end to end where the last point of one curve coincides with the starting point of the next curve. Depending on the application, additional smoothness requirements (such as C1 or C2 continuity) may be added.1

A C0 continuous composite BÃ©zier is also called a polybezier, by similarity to polyline, but whereas in polylines the points are connected by straight lines, in a polybezier the points are connected by BÃ©zier curves. A beziergon (also called bezigon) is a closed path composed of BÃ©zier curves. It is similar to a polygon in that it connects a set of vertices by lines, but whereas in polygons the vertices are connected by straight lines, in a beziergon the vertices are connected by BÃ©zier curves. 234 Some authors even call a C0 composite BÃ©zier curve a "BÃ©zier spline";5 the latter term is however used by other authors as a synonym for the (non-composite) BÃ©zier curve, and they add "composite" in front of "BÃ©zier spline" to denote the composite case.6

Perhaps the most common use of composite BÃ©ziers is to describe the outline of each letter in a PostScript or PDF file. Such outlines are composed of one beziergon for open letters, or multiple beziergons for closed letters. Modern vector graphics and computer font systems like PostScript, Asymptote, Metafont, OpenType, and SVG use composite BÃ©zier curves composed of cubic BÃ©zier curves (3rd order curves) for drawing curved shapes.

Fonts

TrueType fonts use composite BÃ©ziers composed of quadratic BÃ©zier curves (2nd order curves) -- little pieces of parabolas. To describe a typical type design as a computer font to any given accuracy, 3rd order Beziers require less data than 2nd order Beziers; and these in turn require less data than a series of straight lines. This is true even though any one straight line segment requires less data than any one segment of a parabola; and that parabolic segment in turn requires less data than any one segment of a 3rd order curve.
