Overview of the proof

Green and Tao's proof has three main components:

  1. Szemer√©di's theorem, which asserts that subsets of the integers with positive upper density have arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions. It does not a priori apply to the primes because the primes have density zero in the integers.
  2. A transference principle that extends Szemer√©di's theorem to subsets of the integers which are pseudorandom in a suitable sense. Such a result is now called a relative Szemer√©di theorem.
  3. A pseudorandom subset of the integers containing the primes as a dense subset. To construct this set, Green and Tao used ideas from Goldston, Pintz, and Yƒ±ldƒ±rƒ±m's work on prime gaps.3 Once the pseudorandomness of the set is established, the transference principle may be applied, completing the proof.

Numerous simplifications to the argument in the original paper4 have been found. provide a modern exposition of the proof.

Extensions and generalizations

Many of the extensions of Szemer√©di's theorem hold for the primes as well.

Independently, Tao and Ziegler6 and Cook, Magyar, and Titichetrakun78 derived a multidimensional generalization of the Green‚ÄìTao theorem. The Tao‚ÄìZiegler proof was also simplified by Fox and Zhao.9
