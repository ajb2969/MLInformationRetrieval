Head grammar

Head Grammar (HG) is a grammar formalism introduced in Carl Pollard (1984)1 as an extension of the Context-free grammar class of grammars. Head Grammar is therefore a type of phrase structure grammar, as opposed to a dependency grammar. The class of head grammars is a subset of the linear context-free rewriting systems.

One typical way of defining head grammars is to replace the terminal strings of CFGs with indexed terminal strings, where the index denotes the "head" word of the string. Thus, for example, a CF rule such as A → a b c normal-→ A a b c A\to abc might instead be A → ( a b c , 0 ) normal-→ A a b c 0 A\to(abc,0) , where the 0th terminal, the a, is the head of the resulting terminal string. For convenience of notation, such a rule could be written as just the terminal string, with the head terminal denoted by some sort of mark, as in A → a ^ b c normal-→ A normal-^ a b c A\to\widehat{a}bc .

Two fundamental operations are then added to all rewrite rules: wrapping and concatenation.

Form of Rules

Head Grammar rules are defined in terms of these two operations, with rules taking either of the forms

X → w ( α , β ) normal-→ X w α β X\to w(\alpha,\beta) X → c m , n ( α , β , … ) normal-→ X subscript c m n α β normal-… X\to c_{m,n}(\alpha,\beta,...)

where α α \alpha , β β \beta , ... are each either a terminal string or a non-terminal symbol.

Formal Properties

Equivalencies

Vijay-Shanker and Weir (1994)2 demonstrates that Linear Indexed Grammars, Combinatory Categorial Grammars, Tree-adjoining Grammars, and Head Grammars are weakly equivalent formalisms, in that they all define the same string languages.
