}} In probability theory and statistics, the chi distribution is a continuous probability distribution. It is the distribution of the square root of the sum of squares of independent random variables having a standard normal distribution. The most familiar example is the Maxwell distribution of (normalized) molecular speeds which is a chi distribution with 3 degrees of freedom (one for each spatial coordinate). If X i subscript X i X_{i} are k independent, normally distributed random variables with means μ i subscript μ i \mu_{i} and standard deviations σ i subscript σ i \sigma_{i} , then the statistic

is distributed according to the chi distribution. Accordingly, dividing by the mean of the chi distribution (scaled by the square root of n − 1) yields the correction factor in the unbiased estimation of the standard deviation of the normal distribution. The chi distribution has one parameter k k k which specifies the number of degrees of freedom (i.e. the number of X i subscript X i X_{i} ).
