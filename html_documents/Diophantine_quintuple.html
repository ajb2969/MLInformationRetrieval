Diophantine quintuple

In mathematics, a diophantine m-tuple is a set of m positive integers { a 1 , a 2 , a 3 , a 4 , … , a m } subscript a 1 subscript a 2 subscript a 3 subscript a 4 normal-… subscript a m \{a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},a_{4},\ldots,a_{m}\} such that a i a j + 1 subscript a i subscript a j 1 a_{i}a_{j}+1 is a perfect square for any 1 ≤ i < j ≤ m 1 i j m 1\leq i .1

Diophantus himself found the set { 1 16 , 33 16 , 17 4 , 105 16 } 1 16 33 16 17 4 105 16 \left\{\frac{1}{16},\frac{33}{16},\frac{17}{4},\frac{105}{16}\right\} of rationals which has the property that each a i a j + 1 subscript a i subscript a j 1 a_{i}a_{j}+1 is a rational square.2 More recently, sets of six positive rationals have been found.3

The first diophantine quadruple was found by Fermat { 1 , 3 , 8 , 120 } 1 3 8 120 \{1,3,8,120\} .4 It was proved in 1969 by Baker and Davenport 5 that a fifth positive integer cannot be added to this set. However, Euler was able to extend this set by adding the rational number 777480 8288641 777480 8288641 \frac{777480}{8288641} .6

No (integer) diophantine quintuples are known, and it is an open problem whether any exist.7 Dujella has shown that at most a finite number of diophantine quintuples exist.8
