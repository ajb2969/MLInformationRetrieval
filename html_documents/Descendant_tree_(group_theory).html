Descendant tree (group theory)

In mathematics, specifically group theory, a descendant tree is a hierarchical structure for visualizing parent-descendant relations between isomorphism classes of finite groups of prime power order p n superscript p n p^{n} , for a fixed prime number p p p and varying integer exponents n ‚â• 0 n 0 n\geq 0 . Such groups are briefly called finite p-groups. The vertices of a descendant tree are isomorphism classes of finite p-groups.

Additionally to their order p n superscript p n p^{n} , finite p-groups have two further related invariants, the nilpotency class c c c and the coclass r = n - c r n c r=n-c . It turned out that descendant trees of a particular kind, the so-called pruned coclass trees whose infinitely many vertices share a common coclass r r r , reveal a repeating finite pattern. These two crucial properties of finiteness and periodicity admit a characterization of all members of the tree by finitely many parametrized presentations. Consequently, descendant trees play a fundamental role in the classification of finite p-groups. By means of kernels and targets of Artin transfer homomorphisms, descendant trees can be endowed with additional structure.

History

Descendant trees with central quotients as parents (1.) are implicit in P. Hall's 1940 paper 35 about isoclinism of groups. Trees with last non-trivial lower central quotients as parents (2.) were first presented by C. R. Leedham-Green at the International Congress of Mathematicians in Vancouver, 1974 .36 The first extensive tree diagrams have been drawn manually by J. A. Ascione, G. Havas and C. R. Leedham-Green (1977) ,37 by J. A. Ascione (1979) ,38 and by B. Nebelung (1989) .39 In the former two cases, the parent definition by means of the lower exponent-p central series (3.) was adopted in view of computational advantages, in the latter case, where theoretical aspects were focussed, the parents were taken with respect to the usual lower central series (2.).
