Constant factor rule in integration

The constant factor rule in integration is a dual of the constant factor rule in differentiation, and is a consequence of the linearity of integration. It states that a constant factor within an integrand can be separated from the integrand and instead multiplied by the integral. For example, where k is a constant:

∫ k d y d x d x = k ∫ d y d x d x . fragments k d y d x d x k d y d x d x normal-. \int k\frac{dy}{dx}dx=k\int\frac{dy}{dx}dx.\quad

Proof

Start by noticing that, from the definition of integration as the inverse process of differentiation:

y = ∫ d y d x d x . y d y d x d x y=\int\frac{dy}{dx}dx.

Now multiply both sides by a constant k. Since k is a constant it is not dependent on x:

k y = k ∫ d y d x d x . (1) fragments k y k d y d x d x normal-. (1) ky=k\int\frac{dy}{dx}dx.\quad\mbox{(1)}

Take the constant factor rule in differentiation:

d ( k y ) d x = k d y d x . d k y d x k d y d x \frac{d\left(ky\right)}{dx}=k\frac{dy}{dx}.

Integrate with respect to x:

k y = ∫ k d y d x d x . (2) fragments k y k d y d x d x normal-. (2) ky=\int k\frac{dy}{dx}dx.\quad\mbox{(2)}

Now from (1) and (2) we have:

k y = k ∫ d y d x d x k y k d y d x d x ky=k\int\frac{dy}{dx}dx

k y = ∫ k d y d x d x . k y k d y d x d x ky=\int k\frac{dy}{dx}dx.

Therefore:

∫ k d y d x d x = k ∫ d y d x d x . (3) fragments k d y d x d x k d y d x d x normal-. (3) \int k\frac{dy}{dx}dx=k\int\frac{dy}{dx}dx.\quad\mbox{(3)}

Now make a new differentiable function:

u = d y d x . u d y d x u=\frac{dy}{dx}.

Substitute in (3):

∫ k u d x = k ∫ u d x . k u d x k u d x \int kudx=k\int udx.

Now we can re-substitute y for something different from what it was originally:

y = u . y u y=u.\,

So:

∫ k y d x = k ∫ y d x . k y d x k y d x \int kydx=k\int ydx.

This is the constant factor rule in integration.

A special case of this, with k=-1, yields:

∫ - y d x = - ∫ y d x . y d x y d x \int-ydx=-\int ydx.
