Glaeser's continuity theorem

<

hr>In mathematical analysis, Glaeser's continuity theorem, is a characterization of the continuity of the derivative of the square roots of functions of class C 2 superscript C 2 C^{2} . It was introduced in 1963 by Georges Glaeser,1 and was later simplified by Jean Dieudonné.2

The theorem states: Let f : U → \R + normal-: f normal-→ U superscript \R f\ :\ U\rightarrow\R^{+} be a function of class C 2 superscript C 2 C^{2} in an open set U contained in \R n superscript \R n \R^{n} , then f f \sqrt{f} is of class C 1 superscript C 1 C^{1} in U if and only if its partial derivatives of first and second order vanish in the zeros of f.
