Ramanujan‚ÄìPetersson conjecture for automorphic forms

reformulated the Ramanujan‚ÄìPetersson conjecture in terms of automorphic representations for G L ( 2 ) G L 2 GL(2) as saying that the local components of automorphic representations lie in the principal series, and suggested this condition as a generalization of the Ramanujan‚ÄìPetersson conjecture to automorphic forms on other groups. Another way of saying this is that the local components of cusp forms should be tempered. However, several authors found counter-examples for anisotropic groups where the component at infinity was not tempered. and showed that the conjecture was also false even for some quasi-split and split groups, by constructing automorphic forms for the unitary group U ( 2 , 1 ) U 2 1 U(2,1) and the symplectic group S p ( 4 ) S p 4 Sp(4) that are non-tempered almost everywhere, related to the representation .

After the counterexamples were found, suggested that a reformulation of the conjecture should still hold. The current formulation of the generalized Ramanujan conjecture is for a globally generic cuspidal automorphic representation of a connected reductive group, where the generic assumption means that the representation admits a Whittaker model. It states that each local component of such a representation should be tempered. It is an observation due to Langlands that establishing functoriality of symmetric powers of automorphic representations of G L ( n ) G L n GL(n) will give a proof of the Ramanujan‚ÄìPetersson conjecture.

The Ramanujan-Petersson conjecture over global function fields

Drinfeld's proof of the global Langlands correspondence for G L ( 2 ) G L 2 GL(2) over a global function field leads towards a proof of the Ramanujan‚ÄìPetersson conjecture. Lafforgue (2002) successfully extended Drinfeld's shtuka technique to the case of G L ( n ) G L n GL(n) in positive characteristic. Via a different technique that extends the Langlands-Shahidi method to include global function fields, proves the Ramanujan conjecture for the classical groups.

Applications

The most celebrated application of the Ramanujan conjecture is the explicit construction of Ramanujan graphs by Lubotzky, Phillips and Sarnak. Indeed, the name "Ramanujan graph" was derived from this connection. Another application is that the Ramanujan‚ÄìPetersson conjecture for the general linear group G L ( n ) G L n GL(n) implies Selberg's conjecture about eigenvalues of the Laplacian for some discrete groups.
