Preisach model of hysteresis

The Preisach model of hysteresis generalizes hysteresis loops as the parallel connection of independent relay hysterons. It was first suggested in 1935 by Ferenc (Franz) Preisach1 in the German academic journal "Zeitschrift für Physik".2 Since then, it has become a widely accepted model of hysteresis.3 The Preisach model is especially accurate in the field of ferromagnetism, as the ferromagnetic material can be described as a network of small domains, each magnetized to a value of either h h h or - h h -h . A sample of iron, for example, may have evenly distributed magnetic domains, resulting in a net magnetic field of zero.

Discrete Preisach model

The Preisach Model consists of many relay hysterons connected in parallel, given weights, and summed. This is best visualized by a block diagram:

Each of these relays has different ( α , β ) α β (\alpha,\beta) and N N N thresholds and is scaled by N N N . Each relay can be plotted on a so-called Preisach plane with its α β α β \alpha\beta values. Depending on their distribution on the Preisach plane, the relay hysterons can represent hysteresis with good accuracy. As well, with increasing α β α β \alpha\beta , the true hysteresis curve is approximated better.

In the limit as ( α i , β i ) subscript α i subscript β i (\alpha_{i},\beta_{i}) approaches infinity, we obtain the continuous Preisach model.

One of the easiest ways to look at the preisach model is using a geometric interpretation. Consider a plane of coordinates α < β α β \alpha<\beta . On this plane, each point α = β α β \alpha=\beta is mapped to a specific relay hysteron μ ( α , β ) μ α β \mu(\alpha,\beta) .

We consider only the half-plane ( α i , β i ) subscript α i subscript β i (\alpha_{i},\beta_{i}) as any other case does not have a physical equivalent in nature.

Next, we take a specific point on the half plane and build a right triangle by drawing two lines parallel to the axes, both from the point to the line μ ( α , β ) = 0 μ α β 0 \mu(\alpha,\beta)=0 .

We now present the Preisach density function, denoted $\mu(\alpha,\beta)$. This function describes the amount of relay hysterons of each distinct values of $(\alpha_{i},\beta_{i})$. As a default we say that outside the right triangle $\mu(\alpha,\beta)=0$.

A modified formulation of the classical Preisach model has been presented, allowing analitycal expression of the Everett function.4 This makes the model considerably faster and especially adequate for inclusion in electromagnetic field computation or electric circuit analysis codes.
