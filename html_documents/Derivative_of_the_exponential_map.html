Derivative of the exponential map

In the theory of Lie groups, the exponential map is a map from the Lie algebra 𝐠 𝐠 \mathbf{g} of a Lie group G G G into G G G . In case G G G is a matrix Lie group, the exponential map reduces to the matrix exponential. The exponential map, denoted e x p : 𝐠 → G normal-: e x p 𝐠 normal-→ G exp:\mathbf{g}→G , is analytic and has as such a derivative d d t e x p ( X ( t ) ) : T 𝐠 → T G normal-: d d t e x p X t T 𝐠 normal-→ T G \frac{d}{dt}exp(X(t)):T\mathbf{g}→TG , where X ( t ) X t X(t) is a path in the Lie algebra, and a closely related differential d e x p : T 𝐠 → T G normal-: d e x p T 𝐠 normal-→ T G dexp:T\mathbf{g}→TG .1

The formula for d e x p d e x p dexp was first proved by Friedrich Schur (1891).2 It was later elaborated by Henri Poincaré (1899) in the context of the problem of expressing Lie group multiplication using Lie algebraic terms.3 It is also sometimes known as Duhamel's formula.

The formula is important both in pure and applied mathematics. It enters into proofs of theorems such as the Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff formula, and it is used frequently in physics4 for example in quantum field theory, as in the Magnus expansion in perturbation theory, and in lattice gauge theory.
