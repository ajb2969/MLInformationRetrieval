Zodiac P.I.

is a shōjo (targeted towards girls) mystery manga series written and illustrated by Natsumi Ando. Appearing as a serial in the monthly manga magazine Nakayoshi from the April 2001 issue to the January 2003 issue, the chapters were compiled into four bound volumes by Kodansha and published from November 2001 to March 2003. Set in present-day Japan, the series focuses on Lili Hoshizawa, a teenage detective who uses astrology to solve cases.

In North America, Tokyopop licensed the series for an English-language translation, and published it from July 2003 to December 2003, although it has since gone out of print. The series has also been translated into other languages. The series has been generally received as a light entertainment.

Plot

Set in modern-day Japan, the series centers on 13-year-old extrovert , who uses astrology to solve crimes. Her childhood friend, , assists her, after he returns from the United States, where he studied criminal psychology. As Detective Spica, Lili uses the magical star ring, left to her by her missing mother, to aid her in solving crimes by consulting with one of the twelve astral spirits in the ring and learning the victim's horoscope. Her father, , disapproves of her actions. Periodically, she encounters rival private investigator , who tests her mystery-solving skills.

While solving cases, Lili eventually learns from Hiromi's elder sister, , that Hiromi's allergy to girls resulted from her: ten years ago, he waited in the rain for Lili to show after he left her a love letter. Lili encounters , a fortuneteller and hypnotist who kidnaps her father. After Hiromi rescues him, Zeus takes Hiromi captive and demands the star ring in exchange. Realizing that Sirius is her missing mother, Lili frees her from Zeus's hypnosis. Lili learns that her mother disappeared to protect her family from Zeus, who hates her as he believes that she kidnapped and injured his girlfriend. Lili uncovers the truth: his girlfriend feared that he desired the star ring more than her love and asked Sirius to stage a kidnapping and have him decide between her or the star ring. When he hesitated, she was devastated and threw herself off the cliff. After learning this, Zeus loses his desire for the star ring and lets Lili, Hiromi, and Lili's mother leave. Lili accepts Hiromi's love for her. In the conclusion, Lili's mother takes over her fortune-telling business, and Lili continues to solve crimes with Hiromi's assistance.

Development

Manga artist Natsumi Ando enjoyed astrology in her childhood and had a fondness for stars and Greek mythology.12 For File 2, which deals with a mystery in a piano school, Ando researched a local music school.3

Ando included references to Greek mythology in Zodiac P.I. in the names of the astral spirits. The astral spirit of Virgo, Demeter, takes her name from the goddess of the harvest,4 while the astral spirit for Capricorn, Parn, is a reference to the god Pan.5 Astrea, the astral spirit for Libra, is named after the goddess of justice.6 The twin astral spirits for Gemini take their names from twin brothers Castor and Pollux.7 The centaur Chiron is alluded to in the name of the astral spirit for Sagittarius.8 The name of astral spirit for Taurus is a reference to the woman Io.9 The astral spirit for Pisces and the spirit for Aquarius take their names from the goddess of love and Ganymede, respectively.1011 Other references include stars. Ando named Antares, the astral spirit of Scorpio, and Regulus, the astral spirit of Leo, after the Antares and Regulus stars, respectively.1213

Reception

Joseph Dexter of Mania Entertainment praised the series as light entertainment with nice plot twists; he enjoyed the different personalities of the astral spirits.24 In his review of the second volume, Anime News Network's Allen Divers described the series as similar to Detective Conan and wrote that despite the predictable endings, the series was potentially enjoyable for readers interested in astrology and mysteries.25 According to Jason Thompson, the series worked as light and "formulaic" entertainment for a young audience, and the artwork proved easy to understand.26
