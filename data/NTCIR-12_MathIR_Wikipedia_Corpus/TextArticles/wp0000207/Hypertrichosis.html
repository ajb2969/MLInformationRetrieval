Hypertrichosis

Hypertrichosis (also called Ambras syndrome) is an abnormal amount of hair growth over the body;12 extensive cases of hypertrichosis have informally been called werewolf syndrome,3 because the appearance is similar to the werewolf. The two distinct types of hypertrichosis are generalized hypertrichosis, which occurs over the entire body, and localized hypertrichosis, which is restricted to a certain area.4 Hypertrichosis can be either congenital (present at birth) or acquired later in life.56 The excess growth of hair occurs in areas of the skin with the exception of androgen-dependent hair of the pubic area, face, and axillary regions.7

Several circus sideshow performers in the 19th and early 20th centuries, such as Julia Pastrana, had hypertrichosis.8 Many of them worked as freaks and were promoted as having distinct human and animal traits.

Classification

Two methods of classification are used for hypertrichosis.9 One divides them into either generalized versus localized hypertrichosis, while the other divides them into congenital versus acquired.1011

Congenital

Congenital forms of hypertrichosis are caused by genetic mutations, and are extremely rare, unlike acquired forms.12 Congenital hypertrichosis is always present at birth.13

Hypertrichosis lanuginosa

Congenital hypertrichosis lanuginosa is noticeable at birth, with the infant completely covered in thin lanugo hair.14 In normal circumstances, lanugo hair is shed before birth and replaced by vellus hair; however, in a person with congenital hypertrichosis lanuginosa, the lanugo hair remains after birth.15 The palms of the hands, soles of the feet, and mucous membranes are not affected.16 As the person ages, the lanugo hair may thin, leaving only limited areas of hypertrichosis.17

Generalized hypertrichosis

Congenital generalized hypertrichosis causes males to exhibit excessive facial and upper body hair, whereas women exhibit less severe asymmetrical hair distribution.18 The palms, soles, and mucous membranes are not affected.19

Terminal hypertrichosis

Congenital terminal hypertrichosis is characterized by the presence of fully pigmented terminal hair that covers the entire body.20 This condition is usually accompanied by gingival hyperplasia.21 This form is most responsible for the term "werewolf syndrome" because of the thick, dark hair that appears.22 People with this condition are sometimes performers at circuses because of their unusual appearance.23

Circumscribed hypertrichosis

Congenital circumscribed hypertrichosis is associated with the presence of thick vellus hair on the upper extremities.24 Circumscribed signifies this type of hypertrichosis is restricted to certain parts of the body, in this case, the extrasensory surfaces of the upper extremities.25 Hairy elbow syndrome, a type of congenital circumscribed hypertrichosis, shows excessive growth on and around the elbows.26 This type of hypertrichosis is present at birth, becomes more prominent during aging, and regresses at puberty.27

Localized hypertrichosis

Congenital localized hypertrichosis is a localized increase in hair density and length.28

Nevoid hypertrichosis

Nevoid hypertrichosis may be present at birth or appear later in life.29 It features an isolated area of excessive terminal hair and is usually not related to any other diseases.3031

Acquired

Acquired hypertrichosis appears after birth. The multiple causes include the side effects of drugs, associations with cancer, and possible links with eating disorders. Acquired forms can usually be reduced with various treatments.

Hypertrichosis lanuginosa

Acquired hypertrichosis lanuginosa is characterized by rapid growth of lanugo hair, particularly on the face.3233 Hair also appears on the trunk and armpits, while palms and soles are unaffected.34 The excess hair is commonly referred to as malignant down.35 This hair is very fine and unpigmented.36

Generalized hypertrichosis

Acquired generalized hypertrichosis commonly affects the cheeks, upper lip, and chin.3738 This form also affects the forearms and legs, but is less common in these areas. Another deformity associated with acquired generalized hypertrichosis is multiple hairs occupying the same follicle. It may also include abnormal hair growth patterns as what happens to the eyelashes in a condition known as trichiasis.39 Oral minoxidil treatments for hypertension are known to cause this condition. Topical minoxidil used for alopecia causes hair growth in the areas where it is applied, however this hair disappears shortly after discontinuing the use of topical minoxidil.40

Patterned hypertrichosis

Acquired patterned hypertrichosis is an increase in hair growth in a pattern formation. It is similar to acquired generalized hypertrichosis and is a sign of internal malignancy.41

Localized hypertrichosis

Acquired localized hypertrichosis is an increase in hair density and length often secondary to irritation or trauma.42 This form is restricted to certain areas of the body.

Hirsutism

Hypertrichosis is often mistakenly classified as hirsutism.43 Hirsutism is a type of hypertrichosis exclusive to women and children, resulting from an excess of androgen-sensitive hair growth.44 Patients with hirsutism exhibit patterns of adult male hair growth.45 Chest and back hair are often present on women with hirsutism.46

Hirsutism is both congenital and acquired. It is linked to excessive male hormones in women, thus symptoms may include acne, deepening of the voice, irregular menstrual periods, and the formation of a more masculine body shape.47 Increases in androgen (male hormone) levels are the primary cause of most hirsutism cases.48 If caused by increased levels of androgens, it can be treated with medications that reduce androgen levels. Some birth control pills and spironolactone reduce androgen levels.49

Signs and symptoms

The primary characteristic of all forms of hypertrichosis is excessive hair. Hair in hypertrichosis is usually longer than expected50 and may consist of any hair type (lanugo, vellus, or terminal).51 Patterned forms of hypertrichosis cause hair growth in patterns. Generalized forms of hypertrichosis result in hair growth over the entire body. Circumscribed and localized forms lead to hair growth restricted to a certain area.

Cause

Genetic

Hypertrichosis lanuginosa

Congenital hypertrichosis lanuginosa may be caused by an inverse mutation on the 8q chromosome;52 however, it could also be possibly the result of a spontaneous genetic mutation rather than inheritance.53 This form is a dominant autosomal (not located on the sex chromosomes) cutaneous disorder, that affects the skin.54

Generalized hypertrichosis

Congenital generalized hypertrichosis has a dominant pattern of inheritance and has been linked to chromosome xq24-q27.1.5556 An affected female (carrying the hypertrichosis gene) has a 50% chance of passing it to her offspring. An affected male will pass this form of hypertrichosis to his daughters, but never the sons.

Generalized hypertrichosis terminalis

Congenital generalized hypertrichosis terminalis is thought to be caused by genetic changes on chromosome 17 resulting in the addition or removal of millions of nucleotides.57 The gene MAP2K6 may be a factor contributing to this condition. This condition may also be due to the change in the chromosome affecting the transcription of genes.58

Medical conditions

Acquired hypertrichosis lanuginosa is commonly present with cancer.59 This condition is also linked to metabolic disorders, such as anorexia, hormone imbalances, such as hyperthyroidism, or as a side effect of certain drugs.60

Acquired generalized hypertrichosis may be caused by cancer. The resulting hair growth is known as malignant down. The mechanism behind cancer induced hypertrichosis is unknown.61 Oral and topical minoxidil treatments are also known to cause acquired generalized hypertrichosis.62

Medications

Acquired localized hypertrichosis

Minoxidil, a medicine for preventing hair loss, is thought to trigger acquired localized hypertrichosis.63 Medication-induced hair changes are often reversible.64

Other

Unknown causes

The exact genetic mutation that causes congenital circumscribed, localized, and nevoid hypertrichosis is unknown.65

Pathophysiology

A number of mechanisms can lead to hypertrichosis. One cause involves areas of the skin that are transforming from the small vellus type to the larger terminal type.66 This change normally occurs during adolescence, when vellus hair follicles in the underarms and groin grow into terminal hair follicles.67 Hypertrichosis involves this same type of switching, but in areas that do not normally produce terminal hair.68 The mechanisms for this switch are poorly understood.69

Another mechanism involves a change in the hair cycle.70 There are three stages of the hair cycle: the anagen phase (hair growth), the catagen phase (hair follicle death), and the telogen phase (hair shedding).71 If the anagen phase increases beyond what is normal; that region of the body will experience excessive hair growth.72

Diagnosis

Hypertrichosis is diagnosed clinically via the occurrence of hair in excess of what is expected for age, sex, and ethnicity in areas that are not androgen-sensitive.73 The excess can be in the form of excessive length or density and may consist of any hair type (lanugo, vellus, or terminal).74

Management

There is no cure for any congenital forms of hypertrichosis. The treatment for acquired hypertrichosis is based on attempting to address the underlying cause.75 Acquired forms of hypertrichosis have a variety of sources, and are usually treated by removing the factor causing hypertrichosis, e.g. a medication with undesired side-effects. All hypertrichosis, congenital or acquired, can be reduced through hair removal.76 Hair removal treatments are categorized into two principal subdivisions: temporary removal and permanent removal.77 Treatment may have adverse effects by causing scarring, dermatitis, or hypersensitivity.78

Temporary hair removal may last from several hours to several weeks, depending on the method used. These procedures are purely cosmetic.79 Depilation methods, such as trimming, shaving, and depilatories, remove hair to the level of the skin and produce results that last several hours to several days.80 Epilation methods, such as plucking, electrology, waxing, sugaring, threading remove the entire hair from the root, the results lasting several days to several weeks.81

Permanent hair removal uses chemicals, energy of various types, or a combination to target the cells that cause hair growth. Laser hair removal is an effective method of hair removal on hairs that have color. Laser cannot treat white hair. The laser targets the melanin color in the lower 1/3 of the hair follicle, which is the target zone.82 Electrolysis (electrology) uses electrical current, and/or localized heating. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) allows only electrology to use the term "permanent hair removal" because it has been shown to be able treat all colors of hair.

Medication to reduce production of hair is currently under testing. One medicinal option suppresses testosterone by increasing the sex hormone-binding globulin.83 Another controls the overproduction of hair through the regulation of a luteinizing hormone.84

Epidemiology

Congenital forms of hypertrichosis are rare. Only 50 cases of congenital hypertrichosis lanuginosa have been recorded since the Middle Ages,85 and fewer than 100 cases of congenital generalized hypertrichosis have been documented in scientific publications and by the media.86 Congenital generalized hypertrichosis is isolated to one family in Mexico.87 Acquired hypertrichosis and hirsutism are more common.8889 For example, hirsutism occurs in about 10% of women between ages 18 and 45.90

Society and culture

People with hair often found jobs as circus performers, barbers, making the best of their unusual appearance. Fedor Jeftichew ("Jo-Jo the Dog-faced Man"), Stephan Bibrowski ("Lionel the Lion-faced Man"), Jesus "Chuy" Aceves ("Wolfman"), and Annie Jones ("the bearded woman"), all had hypertrichosis.91 Extensive hypertrichosis carries an emotional burden and can cause cosmetic embarrassment; however, some people attempt no treatments because they say it defines who they are.9293

Julia Pastrana

Julia Pastrana (1834–1860)94 traveled throughout the United States in a freak show as the bearded lady, capturing the attention of many artists.95 She is portrayed as having dark extensive hairs distributed equally throughout the surface of her body, even on the palms of her hands.96 Originally, she was believed to have congenital hypertrichosis lanuginosa; however, the generalized form of the syndrome coupled with her gingival hyperlasia indicated that her condition was congenital terminal hypertrichosis.9798 This was not confirmed until after her death, when it became clear that her X-linked syndrome resulted in terminal hairs.99

Supattra Sasupan

In 2011, Supattra Sasupan100 (; born August 5, 2000),101 an 11-year-old girl from Thailand with hypertrichosis was named the world's hairiest girl by the Guinness Book of World Records (as Supatra Sasuphan).102

The hairy family of Burma

One record in history concerning congenital hypertrichosis lanuginosa is the hairy family of Burma, a four-generational pedigree of the disease. In 1826, John Crawford was leading a mission for the Governor-General of India through Burma.103 104He tells of meeting a hairy man, Shwe-Maong. Shwe-Maong lived in the court of King Ava and acted as an entertainer.105 Shwe-Maong had four children: three unaffected children, and one child with congenital hypertrichosis, named Maphoon.106 On a second mission to Ava, Maphoon was described as a thirty-year-old woman with two sons, one of which had hypertrichosis.107 The affected son was named Maong-Phoset. He had an affected daughter named Mah-Me.108 Whereas all affected members of the family had dental problems, the unaffected members had perfect teeth.109

History

The first recorded case of hypertrichosis was Petrus Gonsalvus of the Canary Islands.110 This was documented by Altrovandus in mid 17th century and published in his text Monstrorum Historia cum Paralipomenis historiae omnium animalium published in 1642.111112 He noted two daughters, a son, and a grandchild in Gonsalvus' family all had hypertrichosis. Altrovandus dubbed them the Ambras family, after the Ambras Castle near Innsbruck, where portraits of the family were found.113 During the next 300 years, about 50 cases were observed. The scientist Rudolf Virchow described a form of hypertrichosis accompanied by gingival hyperplasia in 1873.114
