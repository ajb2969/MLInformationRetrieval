Imaginary unit

The term imaginary unit or unit imaginary number refers to a solution to the equation −1}}. As a convention, the solution is usually denoted i i i . Since there is no real number with this property, it extends the real numbers, and under the assumption that the familiar properties of addition and multiplication (namely closure, associativity, commutativity and distributivity) continue to hold for this extension, the complex number system is generated by including it.

Imaginary numbers are an important mathematical concept, which extends the real number system ℝ normal-ℝ ℝ to the complex number system ℂ normal-ℂ ℂ , which in turn provides at least one root for every nonconstant polynomial P ( x ) P x P(x) (see Algebraic closure and Fundamental theorem of algebra). Alternative notations

  • In electrical engineering and related fields, the imaginary unit is often denoted by j j j to avoid confusion with electric current as a function of time, traditionally denoted by i ( t ) i t i(t) or just i i i . The Python programming language also uses j j j to mark the imaginary part of a complex number. MATLAB associates both i i i and j j j with the imaginary unit, although 1 i 1 i 1i or 1 j 1 j 1j is preferable, for speed and improved robustness.3
  • Some texts use the Greek letter iota ( ι ι ι ) for the imaginary unit, to avoid confusion, especially with index and subscripts.
  • Each of i i i , j j j , and k k k is an imaginary unit in the quaternions. In bivectors and biquaternions an additional imaginary unit h h h is used.

Matrices

When 2 × 2 real matrices m are used for a source, and the number one (1) is identified with the identity matrix, and minus one (−1) with the negative of the identity matrix, then there are many solutions to m2 = −1. In fact, there are many solutions to m2 = +1 and m2 = 0 also. Any such m can be taken as a basis vector, along with 1, to form a planar algebra.
