Conjunction introduction

Conjunction introduction (often abbreviated simply as conjunction and also called and introduction123) is a valid rule of inference of propositional logic. The rule makes it possible to introduce a conjunction into a logical proof. It is the inference that if the proposition p is true, and proposition q is true, then the logical conjunction of the two propositions p and q is true. For example, if it's true that it's raining, and it's true that I'm inside, then it's true that "it's raining and I'm inside". The rule can be stated:

P , Q ∴ P and Q P Q therefore absent P italic- and Q \frac{P,Q}{\therefore P\and Q}

where the rule is that wherever an instance of " P P P " and " Q Q Q " appear on lines of a proof, a " P and Q P italic- and Q P\and Q " can be placed on a subsequent line.

Formal notation

The conjunction introduction rule may be written in sequent notation:

P , Q ⊢ P and Q proves P Q P italic- and Q P,Q\vdash P\and Q

where ⊢ proves \vdash is a metalogical symbol meaning that P and Q P italic- and Q P\and Q is a syntactic consequence if P P P and Q Q Q are each on lines of a proof in some logical system;

where P P P and Q Q Q are propositions expressed in some formal system.
